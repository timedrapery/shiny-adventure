# OSF Glossary Profile

## Purpose

This note records a source-backed glossary profile drawn from `OSF Glossary.pdf`.
The glossary mixes Pali, Sanskrit, school terms, and some informal English
teaching shorthand. Only Buddhist lexical headwords are treated as term-entry
candidates for this repository.

## Editorial Rule

When the glossary gives a rendering or explanation that differs from the current
house style:

1. keep the current `preferred_translation` unless there is an explicit
   editorial decision to change it
2. add the glossary wording as an alternate, contextual rendering, or note when
   it is reusable
3. distinguish Buddhist lexical entries from conversational coaching labels
   that do not belong in `terms/`

Within OSF editorial authority, the OSF glossary sits at the same internal
house-material level as named OSF books. It is therefore not an outside
source. It may directly support house defaults, notes, alternates, and intake
decisions, while Dhammarato and Buddhadasa remain the next lineage authorities
under that OSF publication layer.

## Recurring Glossary Tendencies

- `anapanasati` is explained as remembering to look at in-and-out breathing
- `anatta` is paraphrased as no one in charge
- `anicca` is stressed as constant and ever-changing
- `dukkha` is framed as dissatisfaction
- `jhana` is explained as a satisfied unified mind fit for work
- `samadhi` is explained as unification of mind or composure
- `nibbana` is explained as cooling down
- `nirodha` is explained as stopping, ceasing, or ending
- `sati` is treated in present-moment awareness language
- `sankhara` is explained in mental-formation and conditioned-process language
- `upekkha` is explained as dynamic balance
- `vedana` is explained as sensation or feeling from contact

## New-Term Scope

The glossary contains a number of missing Buddhist lexical headwords that fit
this repository and can be added as minor entries, including terms such as
`dana`, `bhikkhuni`, `samatha`, `vipassana`, `samsara`, `mara`, and several
Sanskrit variant forms such as `dharma`, `prajna`, and `marga`.
