# Drift-Risk Terms

This document identifies high-risk doctrinal terms that regularly destabilize translation consistency. These terms should be reviewed with extra care before changing default renderings, context rules, or related compounds.

## Why Drift Happens

Translation drift usually starts when one of the following happens:

- a common English gloss is treated as self-evident when it is not
- a doctrinal term is translated differently across nearby contexts without explanation
- compound forms stop inheriting the headword policy in a predictable way
- explanatory prose quietly becomes the new default rendering

## High-Risk Terms

### `dukkha`

Risk: English tradition often defaults to `suffering`, while this repository uses a broader handling centered on `dissatisfaction` and approved alternates. Drift here spreads immediately into the four noble truths, dependent arising, and practical instruction.

### `saṅkhāra`

Risk: This term is highly context-sensitive and can refer to constructions, fabrications, or conditioned puttings-together depending on frame. It also affects compounds and aggregate language, so unrecorded synonym changes spread quickly.

### `viññāṇa`

Risk: Common English glosses such as `consciousness` may flatten important distinctions or collide with neighboring mind-terms. The term also participates in dependent-arising formulas and sense-base compounds.

### `nibbāna`

Risk: The term is often over-normalized into inherited English religious vocabulary. The repository's treatment must remain explicit so explanatory paraphrase does not silently replace the governed form.

### `sati`

Risk: English-language Buddhism often defaults to `mindfulness`, while this repository prefers `remembering`. This term also drives compounds such as `sammā-sati`, `satipaṭṭhāna`, and `ānāpānasati`.

### `jhāna`

Risk: The term is frequently narrowed or inflated by English preferences such as `absorption`. Drift here affects meditation instructions, staged practice descriptions, and linked factors.

### `samādhi`

Risk: This term is often translated as `concentration`, but the repository prefers `mental composure`. Drift in `samādhi` propagates directly into path factors and meditation-family compounds.

### `saññā`

Risk: The term sits near perception, recognition, labeling, and conceptual marking. Small gloss shifts can blur distinctions with `viññāṇa`, `sati`, and aggregate terminology.

### `āsava`

Risk: The term is easily flattened into generic defilement-language or rotated among `outflow`, `taint`, `effluent`, and older inherited glosses. Drift here weakens `MN 2`, `āsavānaṁ khayā`, and `āsavakkhaya`.

### `upādāna`

Risk: The term often collapses into generic attachment-language. In this repository it must stay distinct from `taṇhā`, preserve its appropriative force, and still allow the fixed `clung-to heaps` expressions to remain stable.

### `anusaya`

Risk: The term can blur into habit-language, broad defilement-language, or active hindrance-language. Drift here damages the repo's ability to distinguish latent persistence from active obstruction and binding fetter.

### `saṁyojana`

Risk: The familiar English `fetter` is stable, but nearby prose easily blurs it with `anusaya`, `āsava`, or vague attachment-language. Drift here destabilizes awakening-stage analysis and lower/higher-fetter handling.

### `kilesa` and `upakkilesa`

Risk: These terms are close enough in English that translators may let the broader defilement family swallow the narrower corruption family. Drift here spreads quickly into `MN 7` and related cleansing-imagery surfaces.

## Editorial Rule

When editing one of the terms above:

1. review the headword entry
2. review core compounds and formula usage
3. check neighboring terms in the same doctrinal family
4. run the validation and drift-checking scripts

These terms should be handled as policy-bearing records, not as isolated glosses.
