# DN 15: Mahānidāna Sutta

## Editorial Note

- This is a repo-native study translation of DN 15, designed to be readable as
  a single Markdown document.
- Companion translator notes:
  [DN 15 Translation Notes](dn15-mahanidana-sutta-notes.md)
- Source basis: the Pali text of DN 15 as segmented in SuttaCentral's Bilara
  data.
- House choices that most affect this sutta: `paṭiccasamuppāda` is rendered
  `dependent arising`; `paccaya` is rendered `condition`; `viññāṇa` is
  rendered `knowing`; `nāmarūpa` is rendered `name-and-form`; `phassa` is
  rendered `contact`; `vedanā` is rendered `felt experience`; `taṇhā` is
  rendered `ignorant wanting`; `upādāna` is rendered `taking personally`;
  `bhava` is rendered `becoming`; `jāti` is rendered `birth`;
  `jarāmaraṇa` is rendered `ageing and dying`; `paññatti` and related
  designation language are rendered with `designate` / `designation`;
  `saññā` is rendered `recognition`; `viññāṇaṭṭhiti` is rendered `station of
  knowing`; `vimokkha` is rendered `release`; `āsava` is rendered `outflow`;
  `cetovimutti` is rendered `release of mind`; `paññāvimutti` is rendered
  `release through discernment`; and `ubhatobhāga-vimutti` is rendered
  `two-sided release`.
- This edition treats DN 15 as a control passage for the reciprocal
  name-and-form / knowing structure, the contact-to-feeling hinge, the
  designation boundary of the round, and the difference between conditioned
  stations of knowing and final release.
- This is the first stable study edition of the repository's DN 15 translation
  surface.

## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying among the Kurus, near a Kuru town named
Kammāsadhamma.

Then Venerable Ānanda approached the Buddha, bowed, and sat to one side.
Sitting to one side, Venerable Ānanda said this to the Buddha:

"Wonderful, bhante. Amazing, bhante.

This dependent arising is deep and appears deep, yet to me it seems very clear
and open."

"Do not say that, Ānanda. Do not say that, Ānanda.

This dependent arising is deep and appears deep.

Because this Dhamma is not understood and not penetrated, this population has
become tangled like string, knotted like a tangle, matted like muñja grass and
reeds, and does not go beyond a woeful state, a bad destination, a downfall
state, and saṃsāra.

### The Conditional Chain

"If, when asked, 'Is there ageing and dying because of this conditionality?'
one should answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there ageing and dying?' one should
answer, 'With birth as condition, ageing and dying.'

If, when asked, 'Is there birth because of this conditionality?' one should
answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there birth?' one should answer, 'With
becoming as condition, birth.'

If, when asked, 'Is there becoming because of this conditionality?' one should
answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there becoming?' one should answer,
'With taking personally as condition, becoming.'

If, when asked, 'Is there taking personally because of this conditionality?'
one should answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there taking personally?' one should
answer, 'With ignorant wanting as condition, taking personally.'

If, when asked, 'Is there ignorant wanting because of this conditionality?' one
should answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there ignorant wanting?' one should
answer, 'With felt experience as condition, ignorant wanting.'

If, when asked, 'Is there felt experience because of this conditionality?' one
should answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there felt experience?' one should
answer, 'With contact as condition, felt experience.'

If, when asked, 'Is there contact because of this conditionality?' one should
answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there contact?' one should answer,
'With name-and-form as condition, contact.'

If, when asked, 'Is there name-and-form because of this conditionality?' one
should answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there name-and-form?' one should
answer, 'With knowing as condition, name-and-form.'

If, when asked, 'Is there knowing because of this conditionality?' one should
answer, 'There is.'

If they ask, 'With what as condition is there knowing?' one should answer,
'With name-and-form as condition, knowing.'

Thus, Ānanda:

with name-and-form as condition, knowing;
with knowing as condition, name-and-form;
with name-and-form as condition, contact;
with contact as condition, felt experience;
with felt experience as condition, ignorant wanting;
with ignorant wanting as condition, taking personally;
with taking personally as condition, becoming;
with becoming as condition, birth;
with birth as condition, ageing and dying, sorrow, lamentation,
dissatisfaction, distress, and despair come to be.

That is how this whole heap of dissatisfaction comes to arise.

### Birth as Condition for Ageing and Dying

"It was said, 'With birth as condition, ageing and dying.'

This is the way to understand how with birth as condition there is ageing and
dying.

If there were no birth at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, in any class of
beings - gods as gods, gandhabbas as gandhabbas, yakkhas as yakkhas, spirits
as spirits, humans as humans, quadrupeds as quadrupeds, birds as birds, or
reptiles as reptiles - if birth into this or that state were absent for those
beings, then with birth wholly absent, with the quenching of birth, would
ageing and dying be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for ageing and dying, namely birth.

### Becoming as Condition for Birth

"It was said, 'With becoming as condition, birth.'

This is the way to understand how with becoming as condition there is birth.

If there were no becoming at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere - sensual
becoming, form becoming, or formless becoming - then with becoming wholly
absent, with the quenching of becoming, would birth be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for birth, namely becoming.

### Taking Personally as Condition for Becoming

"It was said, 'With taking personally as condition, becoming.'

This is the way to understand how with taking personally as condition there is
becoming.

If there were no taking personally at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere -
sensual clinging, taking views personally, clinging to rules and observances,
or taking self-doctrine personally - then with taking personally wholly
absent, with the quenching of taking personally, would becoming be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for becoming, namely taking personally.

### Ignorant Wanting as Condition for Taking Personally

"It was said, 'With ignorant wanting as condition, taking personally.'

This is the way to understand how with ignorant wanting as condition there is
taking personally.

If there were no ignorant wanting at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere -
ignorant wanting for forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tangibles, or dhammas -
then with ignorant wanting wholly absent, with the quenching of ignorant
wanting, would taking personally be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for taking personally, namely ignorant wanting.

### Felt Experience as Condition for Ignorant Wanting

"It was said, 'With felt experience as condition, ignorant wanting.'

This is the way to understand how with felt experience as condition there is
ignorant wanting.

If there were no felt experience at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere -
felt experience born of eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact,
body-contact, or mind-contact - then with felt experience wholly absent, with
the quenching of felt experience, would ignorant wanting be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for ignorant wanting, namely felt experience.

### From Felt Experience to Conflict

"So, Ānanda:

depending on felt experience, there is ignorant wanting;
depending on ignorant wanting, searching;
depending on searching, gain;
depending on gain, evaluation;
depending on evaluation, desire and passion;
depending on desire and passion, fixation;
depending on fixation, possession;
depending on possession, stinginess;
depending on stinginess, guarding.

Because of guarding, many bad, unwholesome dhammas come to be: taking up rods,
taking up weapons, quarrels, fights, disputes, accusations, divisive speech,
and false speech.

"It was said, 'Because of guarding, many bad, unwholesome dhammas come to be:
taking up rods, taking up weapons, quarrels, fights, disputes, accusations,
divisive speech, and false speech.'

This is the way to understand this.

If there were no guarding at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
guarding wholly absent, with the quenching of guarding, would those many bad,
unwholesome dhammas come to be?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for the arising of those many bad, unwholesome dhammas,
namely guarding.

"It was said, 'Depending on stinginess, guarding.'

If there were no stinginess at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
stinginess wholly absent, with the quenching of stinginess, would guarding be
found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for guarding, namely stinginess.

"It was said, 'Depending on possession, stinginess.'

If there were no possession at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
possession wholly absent, with the quenching of possession, would stinginess
be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for stinginess, namely possession.

"It was said, 'Depending on fixation, possession.'

If there were no fixation at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
fixation wholly absent, with the quenching of fixation, would possession be
found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for possession, namely fixation.

"It was said, 'Depending on desire and passion, fixation.'

If there were no desire and passion at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere,
then with desire and passion wholly absent, with the quenching of desire and
passion, would fixation be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for fixation, namely desire and passion.

"It was said, 'Depending on evaluation, desire and passion.'

If there were no evaluation at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
evaluation wholly absent, with the quenching of evaluation, would desire and
passion be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for desire and passion, namely evaluation.

"It was said, 'Depending on gain, evaluation.'

If there were no gain at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with gain
wholly absent, with the quenching of gain, would evaluation be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for evaluation, namely gain.

"It was said, 'Depending on searching, gain.'

If there were no searching at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere, then with
searching wholly absent, with the quenching of searching, would gain be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for gain, namely searching.

"It was said, 'Depending on ignorant wanting, searching.'

If there were no ignorant wanting at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere -
ignorant wanting for sensuality, ignorant wanting for becoming, or ignorant
wanting for non-becoming - then with ignorant wanting wholly absent, with the
quenching of ignorant wanting, would searching be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for searching, namely ignorant wanting.

Thus, Ānanda, these two dhammas meet at the two aspects of felt experience.

### Contact as Condition for Felt Experience

"It was said, 'With contact as condition, felt experience.'

This is the way to understand how with contact as condition there is felt
experience.

If there were no contact at all, in any way, of anyone anywhere - eye-contact,
ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, or mind-contact -
then with contact wholly absent, with the quenching of contact, would felt
experience be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for felt experience, namely contact.

### Name-and-Form as Condition for Contact

"It was said, 'With name-and-form as condition, contact.'

This is the way to understand how with name-and-form as condition there is
contact.

If the features, marks, signs, and indications by which the name-body is
designated were absent, would designation-contact be found in the form-body?"

"No, bhante."

"If the features, marks, signs, and indications by which the form-body is
designated were absent, would resistance-contact be found in the name-body?"

"No, bhante."

"If the features, marks, signs, and indications by which both the name-body and
the form-body are designated were absent, would designation-contact or
resistance-contact be found?"

"No, bhante."

"If the features, marks, signs, and indications by which name-and-form is
designated were absent, would contact be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for contact, namely name-and-form.

### Knowing as Condition for Name-and-Form

"It was said, 'With knowing as condition, name-and-form.'

This is the way to understand how with knowing as condition there is
name-and-form.

If knowing did not descend into the mother's womb, would name-and-form gather
there in the mother's womb?"

"No, bhante."

"If knowing, after descending into the mother's womb, were to depart, would
name-and-form be brought forth into this state of being?"

"No, bhante."

"If the knowing of a young boy or a young girl were cut off while still young,
would name-and-form grow, increase, and reach fullness?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for name-and-form, namely knowing.

### Name-and-Form as Condition for Knowing

"It was said, 'With name-and-form as condition, knowing.'

This is the way to understand how with name-and-form as condition there is
knowing.

If knowing did not gain a footing in name-and-form, would the arising of future
birth, ageing and dying, and dissatisfaction be found?"

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, this is the cause, this is the source, this is the origin,
this is the condition for knowing, namely name-and-form.

To this extent, Ānanda, one may be born, age, die, pass away, or reappear.

To this extent there is the path of designation, the path of expression, the
path of concept, and the field of discernment. To this extent the round turns
for designating this state of being, namely name-and-form together with
knowing, proceeding by mutual conditionality.

### Designating a Self

"And to what extent, Ānanda, does one designate a self?

One designates a self with form and limited:

'My self has form and is limited.'

One designates a self with form and infinite:

'My self has form and is infinite.'

One designates a formless self and limited:

'My self is formless and limited.'

One designates a formless self and infinite:

'My self is formless and infinite.'

"Here, Ānanda, one who designates a self with form and limited either
designates it that way in the present, or designates it that way in the future,
or thinks, 'Although it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of a limited self with form lies
underneath.

One who designates a self with form and infinite either designates it that way
in the present, or designates it that way in the future, or thinks, 'Although
it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of an infinite self with form
lies underneath.

One who designates a formless self and limited either designates it that way in
the present, or designates it that way in the future, or thinks, 'Although it
is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of a limited formless self lies
underneath.

One who designates a formless self and infinite either designates it that way
in the present, or designates it that way in the future, or thinks, 'Although
it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of an infinite formless self lies
underneath.

To this extent, Ānanda, one designates a self.

### Not Designating a Self

"And to what extent, Ānanda, does one not designate a self?

One does not designate a self with form and limited:

'My self has form and is limited.'

One does not designate a self with form and infinite:

'My self has form and is infinite.'

One does not designate a formless self and limited:

'My self is formless and limited.'

One does not designate a formless self and infinite:

'My self is formless and infinite.'

"Here, Ānanda, one who does not designate a self with form and limited does not
designate it that way in the present, does not designate it that way in the
future, and does not think, 'Although it is not like that, I will make it be
like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of a limited self with form does
not lie underneath.

One who does not designate a self with form and infinite does not designate it
that way in the present, does not designate it that way in the future, and does
not think, 'Although it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of an infinite self with form
does not lie underneath.

One who does not designate a formless self and limited does not designate it
that way in the present, does not designate it that way in the future, and does
not think, 'Although it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of a limited formless self does
not lie underneath.

One who does not designate a formless self and infinite does not designate it
that way in the present, does not designate it that way in the future, and does
not think, 'Although it is not like that, I will make it be like that.'

In this case, it is enough to say that a view of an infinite formless self does
not lie underneath.

To this extent, Ānanda, one does not designate a self.

### Regarding a Self

"And to what extent, Ānanda, does one regard a self?

One regards felt experience as self:

'Felt experience is my self.'

Or one regards self in this way:

'Felt experience is not my self. My self does not feel.'

Or one regards self in this way:

'Felt experience is not my self. Nor is my self without felt experience. My
self feels, for my self has the nature of felt experience.'

"Here, Ānanda, when someone says:

'Felt experience is my self,'

they should be told:

'Friend, there are these three feelings: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and
mixed feeling. Which of these three feelings do you regard as self?'

At a time when one feels pleasant feeling, one does not at that time feel
painful feeling or mixed feeling. At that time one feels only pleasant feeling.

At a time when one feels painful feeling, one does not at that time feel
pleasant feeling or mixed feeling. At that time one feels only painful feeling.

At a time when one feels mixed feeling, one does not at that time feel pleasant
feeling or painful feeling. At that time one feels only mixed feeling.

Pleasant feeling, Ānanda, is impermanent, conditioned, dependently arisen,
liable to wearing away, passing away, fading, and quenching.

Painful feeling is impermanent, conditioned, dependently arisen, liable to
wearing away, passing away, fading, and quenching.

Mixed feeling is impermanent, conditioned, dependently arisen, liable to
wearing away, passing away, fading, and quenching.

Feeling pleasant feeling, one thinks, 'This is my self.'

With the quenching of that very pleasant feeling, one thinks, 'My self has gone
away.'

Feeling painful feeling, one thinks, 'This is my self.'

With the quenching of that very painful feeling, one thinks, 'My self has gone
away.'

Feeling mixed feeling, one thinks, 'This is my self.'

With the quenching of that very mixed feeling, one thinks, 'My self has gone
away.'

Thus, in this very life, one who says, 'Felt experience is my self,' regards
self as impermanent, mixed with pleasant and painful experience, and liable to
arising and passing away.

Therefore, Ānanda, it does not fit to regard felt experience as self.

"Here, Ānanda, when someone says:

'Felt experience is not my self. My self does not feel,'

they should be told:

'Friend, where there is no felt experience at all, could there be this:
"I am this"?'

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, it does not fit to regard self in this way: 'Felt
experience is not my self. My self does not feel.'

"Here, Ānanda, when someone says:

'Felt experience is not my self. Nor is my self without felt experience. My
self feels, for my self has the nature of felt experience,'

they should be told:

'Friend, if felt experience were wholly and completely quenched without
remainder, then with felt experience wholly absent, with the quenching of felt
experience, could there be this: "I am this"?'

"No, bhante."

"Therefore, Ānanda, it does not fit to regard self in this way: 'Felt
experience is not my self. Nor is my self without felt experience. My self
feels, for my self has the nature of felt experience.'

### Released from the Self-Views

"When, Ānanda, a bhikkhu does not regard felt experience as self, does not
regard a self that does not feel, and does not regard self in this way - 'My
self feels, for my self has the nature of felt experience' - then, not
regarding in this way, one does not take anything personally in the world.

Not taking anything personally, one is not agitated.

Not agitated, one personally reaches final nibbāna.

One discerns:

'Birth is worn away. The holy life has been lived. What had to be done has
been done. There is no more of this state of being.'

"If someone were to say of a bhikkhu whose mind is released in this way, 'It is
their view that the Tathāgata exists after death,' that would be unsuitable.

If someone were to say, 'It is their view that the Tathāgata does not exist
after death,' that would be unsuitable.

If someone were to say, 'It is their view that the Tathāgata both exists and
does not exist after death,' that would be unsuitable.

If someone were to say, 'It is their view that the Tathāgata neither exists nor
does not exist after death,' that would be unsuitable.

Why is that?

Whatever extent there is of designation and the path of designation, whatever
extent there is of expression and the path of expression, whatever extent
there is of concept and the path of concept, whatever extent there is of
discernment and the field of discernment, whatever extent there is of the
round and the turning of the round - from all that the bhikkhu is released
through higher knowing.

To say of a bhikkhu released through higher knowing from all that, 'It is their
view that they do not know and do not see,' would be unsuitable.

### The Seven Stations of Knowing

"Ānanda, there are seven stations of knowing and two fields.

What are the seven?

There are beings diverse in body and diverse in recognition, such as humans,
some devas, and some beings in downfall states.

This is the first station of knowing.

There are beings diverse in body and unified in recognition, such as the
Brahmā-company devas newly arisen.

This is the second station of knowing.

There are beings unified in body and diverse in recognition, such as the
Abhassara devas.

This is the third station of knowing.

There are beings unified in body and unified in recognition, such as the
Subhakiṇha devas.

This is the fourth station of knowing.

There are beings who, by completely going beyond recognitions of form, with the
ending of recognitions of resistance, and not attending to recognitions of
diversity, have gone to the sphere of boundless space, aware that 'space is
boundless.'

This is the fifth station of knowing.

There are beings who, by completely going beyond the sphere of boundless space,
have gone to the sphere of boundless knowing, aware that 'knowing is
boundless.'

This is the sixth station of knowing.

There are beings who, by completely going beyond the sphere of boundless
knowing, have gone to the sphere of nothingness, aware that 'there is nothing.'

This is the seventh station of knowing.

The field of non-recognizing beings and the sphere of
neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition are the two fields.

"Here, Ānanda, there is the first station of knowing: diverse in body and
diverse in recognition, such as humans, some devas, and some beings in downfall
states.

If one discerns this, discerns its arising, discerns its vanishing, discerns
its gratification, discerns its danger, and discerns its escape, is it fit to
delight in it?"

"No, bhante."

"The same pattern applies to the remaining stations of knowing.

And here, Ānanda, there is the field of non-recognizing beings.

If one discerns this, discerns its arising, discerns its vanishing, discerns
its gratification, discerns its danger, and discerns its escape, is it fit to
delight in it?"

"No, bhante."

"And here, Ānanda, there is the sphere of
neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition.

If one discerns this, discerns its arising, discerns its vanishing, discerns
its gratification, discerns its danger, and discerns its escape, is it fit to
delight in it?"

"No, bhante."

"When, Ānanda, a bhikkhu has known as they have come to be the arising,
vanishing, gratification, danger, and escape of these seven stations of knowing
and these two fields, and is released through not taking personally, this
bhikkhu is called one released through discernment.

### The Eight Releases

"Ānanda, there are these eight releases.

What are the eight?

Having form, one sees forms.

This is the first release.

Not recognizing form internally, one sees forms externally.

This is the second release.

One is intent only on the beautiful.

This is the third release.

By completely going beyond recognitions of form, with the ending of
recognitions of resistance, and not attending to recognitions of diversity, one
enters and remains in the sphere of boundless space, aware that 'space is
boundless.'

This is the fourth release.

By completely going beyond the sphere of boundless space, one enters and
remains in the sphere of boundless knowing, aware that 'knowing is boundless.'

This is the fifth release.

By completely going beyond the sphere of boundless knowing, one enters and
remains in the sphere of nothingness, aware that 'there is nothing.'

This is the sixth release.

By completely going beyond the sphere of nothingness, one enters and remains in
the sphere of neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition.

This is the seventh release.

By completely going beyond the sphere of
neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition, one enters and remains in the
cessation of recognition and felt experience.

This is the eighth release.

These are the eight releases.

"When, Ānanda, a bhikkhu enters these eight releases forward, enters them in
reverse, enters them forward and reverse, and, wherever one wishes, however one
wishes, and as long as one wishes, enters them and emerges from them;

and when, through the wearing away of the outflows, one personally realizes the
outflow-free release of mind and release through discernment in this very life,
and enters and remains in it:

this, Ānanda, is called a bhikkhu with two-sided release.

There is no other two-sided release higher or more refined than this two-sided
release."

That is what the Buddha said.

Venerable Ānanda was satisfied and welcomed what the Buddha had said.
