## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Bārāṇasī, at Isipatana, in the Deer
Park.

There the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus."

"Bhante," they replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Buddha Points to Sāriputta and Moggallāna

"Bhikkhus, at Isipatana, in the Deer Park near Bārāṇasī, the Tathāgata, the
arahant, the fully awakened Buddha, set rolling the unsurpassed wheel of
Dhamma that cannot be rolled back by any samaṇa or brahmin or deva or Māra or
Brahmā or anyone in the world. That is:
the explaining, teaching, laying out, establishing, opening up, analyzing, and
making clear of the four noble truths.

And which four?

The explaining, teaching, laying out, establishing, opening up, analyzing, and
making clear of the noble truth of dissatisfaction, the noble truth of the
origin of dissatisfaction, the noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction,
and the noble truth of the path of practice leading to the quenching of
dissatisfaction.

Bhikkhus, stay close to Sāriputta and Moggallāna. Keep company with them.
Those wise bhikkhus support their spiritual companions.

Sāriputta is like someone who gives birth, and Moggallāna is like someone who raises
what has been born.

Sāriputta trains people in the fruit of stream-entry, and Moggallāna in the
highest aim.

Bhikkhus, Sāriputta is able to explain, teach, lay out, establish, open up,
analyze, and make clear the four noble truths in detail."

That is what the Buddha said. Having said this, the Buddha rose from his seat
and entered his dwelling.

### Sāriputta Begins the Analysis

Then, not long after the Buddha had gone, Venerable Sāriputta addressed the
bhikkhus:

"Friends, bhikkhus."

"Friend," they replied.

Venerable Sāriputta said this:

"Friends, at Isipatana, in the Deer Park near Bārāṇasī, the Tathāgata, the
arahant, the fully awakened Buddha, set rolling the unsurpassed wheel of
Dhamma that cannot be rolled back by any samaṇa or brahmin or deva or Māra or
Brahmā or anyone in the world. That is:
the explaining, teaching, laying out, establishing, opening up, analyzing, and
making clear of the four noble truths.

And which four?

### The Noble Truth of Dissatisfaction

And what, friends, is the noble truth of dissatisfaction?

Birth is dissatisfaction. Ageing is dissatisfaction. Death is dissatisfaction.
Sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair are dissatisfaction. Not
getting what one wants is also dissatisfaction. In brief, the five clung-to
heaps are dissatisfaction.

And what, friends, is birth?

Whatever birth, being born, descent, appearance, manifestation of the heaps,
and obtaining of the fields there is for the various beings in the various
classes of beings: this is called birth.

And what, friends, is ageing?

Whatever ageing, wearing out, brokenness of teeth, greying of hair, wrinkling
of skin, decline of life, and maturing of the faculties there is for the
various beings in the various classes of beings: this is called ageing.

And what, friends, is death?

Whatever passing away, vanishing, breaking up, disappearance, dying, death,
time-ending, breakup of the heaps, laying down of the corpse, and cutting off
of the life faculty there is for the various beings in the various classes of
beings: this is called death.

And what, friends, is sorrow?

When someone is affected by some loss or struck by some dissatisfactory thing,
the sorrowing, grieving, inner sorrow, and inner grief that arise: this is
called sorrow.

And what, friends, is lamentation?

When someone is affected by some loss or struck by some dissatisfactory thing,
the crying out, lamenting, and wailing that arise: this is called lamentation.

And what, friends, is pain?

Whatever bodily pain, bodily unpleasantness, or painful unpleasant feeling born
of body-contact there is: this is called pain.

And what, friends, is distress?

Whatever mental pain, mental unpleasantness, or painful unpleasant feeling born
of mind-contact there is: this is called distress.

And what, friends, is despair?

When someone is affected by some loss or struck by some dissatisfactory thing,
the exhaustion, despair, and desperate affliction that arise: this is called
despair.

And what, friends, is 'not getting what one wants is also dissatisfaction'?

For beings subject to birth, this wish arises:
'Oh, if only we were not subject to birth, and birth would not come to us.'
But that is not gained by wishing.
This too is 'not getting what one wants is also dissatisfaction.'

For beings subject to ageing, to illness, to death, and to sorrow,
lamentation, pain, distress, and despair, the same wish arises:
'Oh, if only this would not come to us.'
But that is not gained by wishing.
This too is 'not getting what one wants is also dissatisfaction.'

And what, friends, are the five clung-to heaps that in brief are
dissatisfaction?

They are:

- the clung-to heap of form
- the clung-to heap of felt experience
- the clung-to heap of recognition
- the clung-to heap of putting-together activities
- the clung-to heap of knowing

These are called the five clung-to heaps that in brief are dissatisfaction.

This is called the noble truth of dissatisfaction.

### The Noble Truth of the Origin of Dissatisfaction

And what, friends, is the noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction?

It is that ignorant wanting that leads to renewed becoming, accompanied by
relishing and passion, delighting here and there. That is:

- wanting for sensuality
- wanting for becoming
- wanting for unbecoming

This is called the noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction.

### The Noble Truth of the Quenching of Dissatisfaction

And what, friends, is the noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction?

It is the complete fading and quenching of that very ignorant wanting: giving
it up, relinquishing it, freedom, non-attachment.

This is called the noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction.

### The Noble Truth of the Path

And what, friends, is the noble truth of the path of practice leading to the
quenching of dissatisfaction?

It is just this noble eightfold path:

- right view
- right attitude
- right speech
- right action
- right livelihood
- right effort
- right remembering
- right mental composure

#### Right View

And what, friends, is right view?

Knowledge of dissatisfaction, knowledge of the origin of dissatisfaction,
knowledge of the quenching of dissatisfaction, and knowledge of the path of
practice leading to the quenching of dissatisfaction. This is called right
view.

#### Right Attitude

And what, friends, is right attitude?

An attitude of renunciation, an attitude of non-ill-will, and an attitude of
non-harming. This is called right attitude.

#### Right Speech

And what, friends, is right speech?

Abstaining from false speech, abstaining from divisive speech, abstaining from
harsh speech, and abstaining from idle chatter. This is called right speech.

#### Right Action

And what, friends, is right action?

Abstaining from killing living beings, abstaining from taking what is not
given, and abstaining from sexual misconduct. This is called right action.

#### Right Livelihood

And what, friends, is right livelihood?

Here a noble disciple, having given up wrong livelihood, earns a living by
right livelihood. This is called right livelihood.

#### Right Effort

And what, friends, is right effort?

Here a bhikkhu arouses desire, makes effort, stirs up energy, steadies the
feeling mind, and strives:

- so that unarisen harmful, unwholesome qualities do not arise
- so that arisen harmful, unwholesome qualities are abandoned
- so that unarisen wholesome qualities arise
- so that arisen wholesome qualities remain, do not fade, grow, expand,
  develop, and come to fulfillment

This is called right effort.

#### Right Remembering

And what, friends, is right remembering?

Here a bhikkhu remains observing the body in relation to the body, ardent,
with clear knowing, with remembering, having removed coveting and distress
regarding the world.

One remains observing felt experience in relation to felt experience, ardent,
with clear knowing, with remembering, having removed coveting and distress
regarding the world.

One remains observing the feeling mind in relation to the feeling mind,
ardent, with clear knowing, with remembering, having removed coveting and
distress regarding the world.

One remains observing dhammas among dhammas, ardent, with clear knowing, with
remembering, having removed coveting and distress regarding the world.

This is called right remembering.

#### Right Mental Composure

And what, friends, is right mental composure?

Here a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensuality, secluded from unwholesome
dhammas, enters and remains in the first mental theme, with thinking and
pondering, with delight and ease born of seclusion.

With the subsiding of thinking and pondering, with inner clarity and
unification of mind, one enters and remains in the second mental
theme, without thinking and pondering, with delight and ease born of mental
composure.

With the fading of delight, one remains in dynamic balance, remembering and
clearly knowing, and experiences ease in the body. One enters and remains in
the third mental theme, of which the noble ones say:
'One of dynamic balance and remembering stays at ease.'

With the abandoning of ease and discomfort, and with the earlier ending of
gladness and sadness, one enters and remains in the fourth mental theme,
without discomfort and without ease, purified by dynamic balance and
remembering.

This is called right mental composure.

This is called the noble truth of the path of practice leading to the
quenching of dissatisfaction."

That is what Venerable Sāriputta said. The bhikkhus were glad and welcomed
what Venerable Sāriputta had said.
