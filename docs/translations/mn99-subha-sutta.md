## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, in
Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery.

At that time the student Subha, Todeyya's son, was staying in Sāvatthī in the
house of a certain householder on some business.

Then Subha said to that householder:

"Householder, I have heard that Sāvatthī is not without arahants. Which
ascetic or brahmin should we visit today?"

"Sir, the Buddha is staying near Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, in
Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery. Visit the Buddha."

Agreeing with that householder, Subha went to the Buddha. Having approached,
he exchanged greetings with the Buddha. When the polite and courteous talk was
finished, he sat down to one side and said to the Buddha:

### Householders and Renunciates

"Master Gotama, the brahmins say:

'A householder succeeds in this skillful dhamma. A renunciate does not
succeed in this skillful dhamma.'

What does Master Gotama say about this?"

"On this point, student, I analyze. I do not speak one-sidedly.

I do not praise wrong practice in either householders or renunciates. Because
of wrong practice, neither householders nor renunciates succeed in this
skillful dhamma.

I praise right practice in both householders and renunciates. Because of right
practice, both householders and renunciates succeed in this skillful dhamma."

### Work With Many Or Few Responsibilities

"Master Gotama, the brahmins say:

'The work of household life has many responsibilities, many duties, many
claims, and many undertakings, so it bears great fruit. The work of the
renunciate has few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings, so it does not bear great fruit.'

What does Master Gotama say about this?"

"On this point too, student, I analyze. I do not speak one-sidedly.

Some work has many responsibilities, many duties, many claims, and many
undertakings, and when it fails it bears little fruit.

Some work has many responsibilities, many duties, many claims, and many
undertakings, and when it succeeds it bears great fruit.

Some work has few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings, and when it fails it bears little fruit.

Some work has few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings, and when it succeeds it bears great fruit.

And what is work with many responsibilities, many duties, many claims, and
many undertakings that, when it fails, bears little fruit? Farming.

And what is work with many responsibilities, many duties, many claims, and
many undertakings that, when it succeeds, bears great fruit? Farming.

And what is work with few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings that, when it fails, bears little fruit? Trade.

And what is work with few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings that, when it succeeds, bears great fruit? Trade.

Student, household life is like farming: it carries many responsibilities, and
when it fails it bears little fruit, but when it succeeds it bears great
fruit.

The renunciate life is like trade: it carries few responsibilities, and when
it fails it bears little fruit, but when it succeeds it bears great fruit."

### The Brahmins' Five Grounds

"Master Gotama, the brahmins lay down five dhammas for making merit and for
succeeding in what is skillful."

"If you do not mind, explain them in this assembly."

"It is no trouble when good people such as you are sitting here. Then listen,
student."

"Master Gotama, the brahmins lay down truth as the first dhamma for making
merit and for succeeding in what is skillful. They lay down austerity as the
second. They lay down chaste conduct as the third. They lay down recitation as
the fourth. They lay down generosity as the fifth. These are the five dhammas
that the brahmins lay down for making merit and for succeeding in what is
skillful. What does Master Gotama say about this?"

"Student, is there even one brahmin who says:

'I declare the result of these five dhammas after realizing it for myself by
direct knowing'?"

"No, Master Gotama."

"Is there even one teacher of the brahmins, or a teacher's teacher, or anyone
back through seven generations of teachers, who says:

'I declare the result of these five dhammas after realizing it for myself by
direct knowing'?"

"No, Master Gotama."

"What about the ancient brahmin seers, namely Aṭṭhaka, Vāmaka, Vāmadeva,
Vessāmitta, Yamadaggi, Aṅgīrasa, Bhāradvāja, Vāseṭṭha, Kassapa, and Bhagu, the
authors and transmitters of the hymns that were composed, recited, and handed
down in ancient times, and that the brahmins today still recite and teach?
Did even they say:

'We declare the result of these five dhammas after realizing it for ourselves
by direct knowing'?"

"No, Master Gotama."

"So, student, it turns out that there is not even one brahmin, nor anyone back
through seven generations of teachers, nor even the ancient brahmin seers,
who says:

'We declare the result of these five dhammas after realizing it for ourselves
by direct knowing.'

Suppose there were a line of blind men, each holding on to the one in front.
The first does not see, the middle does not see, and the last does not see.
In the same way, student, the statement of the brahmins seems to me like a
line of blind men. The first does not see, the middle does not see, and the
last does not see."

### The Blind-From-Birth Simile

When this was said, Subha, Todeyya's son, became angry and displeased because
the Buddha had compared the brahmins to a line of blind men. Criticizing and
taunting the Buddha, he said:

"The ascetic Gotama will be shown up!

Master Gotama, the brahmin Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the Subhaga grove says:

'This is exactly how it is with some ascetics and brahmins who claim a
distinction in knowledge and vision beyond the human, worthy of the noble
ones. Their statement turns out to be only laughable, only a name, empty and
hollow. For how could a human being know, see, or directly realize a
distinction in knowledge and vision beyond the human, worthy of the noble
ones? That is not possible.'"

"Student, does the brahmin Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the Subhaga grove know the
minds of all those ascetics and brahmins by encompassing them with his own
mind?"

"Master Gotama, Pokkharasāti does not even know the mind of his own servant
girl Puṇṇikā by encompassing it with his mind. How then could he know the
minds of all those ascetics and brahmins?"

"Suppose, student, a man blind from birth could not see dark and bright
forms, nor blue forms, nor yellow forms, nor red forms, nor crimson forms. He
could not see level ground and uneven ground. He could not see the stars, the
moon, or the sun. He might say:

'There are no dark and bright forms, and no one who sees dark and bright
forms. There are no blue forms, no yellow forms, no red forms, no crimson
forms. There is no level ground and no uneven ground. There are no stars, no
moon, and no sun. I do not know these things. I do not see these things.
Therefore they do not exist.'

Would he be speaking rightly?"

"No, Master Gotama. There are dark and bright forms, and someone who sees
them. There are blue forms, yellow forms, red forms, crimson forms, level
ground and uneven ground, stars, moon, and sun, and someone who sees them. So
it would not be right to say: 'I do not know them. I do not see them.
Therefore they do not exist.'"

"In the same way, student, Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the Subhaga grove is blind
and without sight. It is impossible for him to know, see, or directly realize
a distinction in knowledge and vision beyond the human, worthy of the noble
ones.

What do you think, student? Among those wealthy Kosalan brahmins, namely
Caṅkī, Tārukkha, Pokkharasāti, Jānussoṇi, and your father Todeyya, which is
better: speech that is in agreement or speech that is not in agreement?"

"Speech that agrees, Master Gotama."

"Which is better: speech that is considered or speech that is inconsiderate?"

"Speech that is considered, Master Gotama."

"Which is better: speech spoken after reflection or speech spoken without
reflection?"

"Speech spoken after reflection, Master Gotama."

"Which is better: beneficial speech or useless speech?"

"Beneficial speech, Master Gotama."

"What do you think, student? If that is so, did Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the
Subhaga grove speak in agreement or not in agreement?"

"Not in agreement, Master Gotama."

"Did he speak in a considered way or an inconsiderate way?"

"In an inconsiderate way, Master Gotama."

"Did he speak after reflection or without reflection?"

"Without reflection, Master Gotama."

"Did he speak beneficially or uselessly?"

"Uselessly, Master Gotama."

### The Five Distractions

"Student, there are these five distractions. What five? The sensual
distraction, the aversive distraction, the dull distraction, the agitated
distraction, and the uncertain distraction. These are the five distractions.

Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the Subhaga grove is wrapped up, shut in, flooded
over, and covered by these five distractions. It is impossible for him to
know, see, or directly realize a distinction in knowledge and vision beyond
the human, worthy of the noble ones.

There are these five kinds of sensual stimulation. What five? Visible forms
knowable by the eye, sounds knowable by the ear, smells knowable by the nose,
tastes knowable by the tongue, and tangibles knowable by the body that are
likable, attractive, agreeable, pleasing, tied to sensuality, and passion
stirring. These are the five kinds of sensual stimulation.

Pokkharasāti Opamañña of the Subhaga grove enjoys these five kinds of sensual
stimulation while tied to them, infatuated with them, sunk into them, blind to
the danger in them, and not understanding the escape from them. It is
impossible for him to know, see, or directly realize a distinction in
knowledge and vision beyond the human, worthy of the noble ones."

### Delight Apart From Sensuality

"What do you think, student? Which fire would have the better flame, color,
and radiance: one burning dependent on grass and wood as fuel, or one burning
without depending on grass and wood as fuel?"

"Master Gotama, if it were possible for a fire to burn without depending on
grass and wood as fuel, that fire would have the better flame, color, and
radiance."

"But, student, apart from one with psychic power, that is impossible.

Just as a fire burns dependent on grass and wood as fuel, in the same way I
speak of the delight that depends on the five kinds of sensual stimulation.

And just as a fire burns without depending on grass and wood as fuel, in the
same way I speak of the delight that is apart from sensuality and apart from
unskillful dhammas.

And what is the delight that is apart from sensuality and apart from
unskillful dhammas?

Here, student, a bhikkhu, quite secluded from sensuality, secluded from
unskillful dhammas, enters and remains in the first mental theme, with
thinking and pondering, with delight and ease born of seclusion. This too is
delight apart from sensuality and apart from unskillful dhammas.

Again, with the subsiding of thinking and pondering, with inner clarity and
unification of mind, a bhikkhu enters and remains in the second mental theme,
without thinking and pondering, with delight and ease born of mental
composure. This too is delight apart from sensuality and apart from
unskillful dhammas."

### A Sixth Ground For Merit

"Of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit and for
succeeding in what is skillful, which do they say bears the greatest fruit?"

"Generosity, Master Gotama."

"What do you think, student? Suppose a brahmin were preparing a great
sacrifice. Then two brahmins came wanting to take part in it. One of them
might think:

'If only I myself would get the best seat, the best water, and the best
serving of food in the dining hall, and no other brahmin would get them.'

But it may happen that another brahmin gets the best seat, the best water, and
the best serving of food in the dining hall. Then he thinks:

'Another brahmin gets the best seat, the best water, and the best serving of
food, while I do not.'

So he becomes angry and displeased.

What result do the brahmins lay down for that?"

"Master Gotama, brahmins do not give gifts thinking:

'Let another become angry and displeased because of this.'

Rather, they give only with sympathy."

"If that is so, student, then this becomes a sixth ground for making merit for
the brahmins: namely, what is of the nature of sympathy."

"If that is so, Master Gotama, then this becomes a sixth ground for making
merit for the brahmins: namely, what is of the nature of sympathy."

### Found More Among Renunciates

"Of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit and for
succeeding in what is skillful, where do you see them more often, among
householders or among renunciates?"

"Master Gotama, of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making
merit and for succeeding in what is skillful, I see them more often among
renunciates and only a little among householders.

For a householder has many responsibilities, many duties, many claims, and
many undertakings, and is not constantly and consistently truthful. But a
renunciate has few responsibilities, few duties, few claims, and few
undertakings, and is constantly and consistently truthful.

A householder is not constantly and consistently austere, living chaste
conduct, devoted to recitation, and abundant in generosity. But a renunciate
is constantly and consistently austere, living chaste conduct, devoted to
recitation, and abundant in generosity.

So of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit and for
succeeding in what is skillful, I see them more often among renunciates and
only a little among householders."

### Supports For A Free Mind

"Student, of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit
and for succeeding in what is skillful, I call them supports of the mind for
developing a mind free of enmity and ill will.

Here, student, a bhikkhu is truthful. Thinking, 'I am truthful,' he gains a
sense of the meaning, a sense of the dhamma, and gladness connected with the
dhamma. That gladness connected with what is skillful, I call a support of the
mind for developing a mind free of enmity and ill will.

Again, a bhikkhu is austere. Thinking, 'I am austere,' he gains a sense of the
meaning, a sense of the dhamma, and gladness connected with the dhamma. That
gladness connected with what is skillful, I call a support of the mind for
developing a mind free of enmity and ill will.

Again, a bhikkhu is living chaste conduct. Thinking, 'I am living chaste
conduct,' he gains a sense of the meaning, a sense of the dhamma, and gladness
connected with the dhamma. That gladness connected with what is skillful, I
call a support of the mind for developing a mind free of enmity and ill will.

Again, a bhikkhu is devoted to recitation. Thinking, 'I am devoted to
recitation,' he gains a sense of the meaning, a sense of the dhamma, and
gladness connected with the dhamma. That gladness connected with what is
skillful, I call a support of the mind for developing a mind free of enmity
and ill will.

Again, a bhikkhu is abundant in generosity. Thinking, 'I am abundant in
generosity,' he gains a sense of the meaning, a sense of the dhamma, and
gladness connected with the dhamma. That gladness connected with what is
skillful, I call a support of the mind for developing a mind free of enmity
and ill will.

So, student, of those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit
and for succeeding in what is skillful, I call them supports of the mind for
developing a mind free of enmity and ill will."

### The Path To Company With Divinity

When this was said, Subha said to the Buddha:

"Master Gotama, I have heard:

'The ascetic Gotama knows the path to company with divinity.'"

"What do you think, student? Is the village of Naḷakāra near here, not far
from here?"

"Yes, Master Gotama, the village of Naḷakāra is near here, not far from here."

"What do you think, student? Suppose someone were born and raised in Naḷakāra.
If, as soon as he had gone out from Naḷakāra, people asked him the road to
Naḷakāra, would there be any hesitation or confusion in him when asked?"

"No, Master Gotama."

"Why is that?"

"Because he was born and raised in Naḷakāra. All the roads to Naḷakāra are
well known to him."

"Student, although there might be hesitation or confusion in such a man when
asked the road to Naḷakāra, there is no hesitation or confusion in the
Tathāgata when asked about the realm of divinity or the practice leading to
the realm of divinity.

Student, I know divinity, I know the realm of divinity, and I know the
practice that leads to the realm of divinity. And I know the one who, by
practicing in that way, is reborn in the realm of divinity."

"Master Gotama, I have heard:

'The ascetic Gotama teaches the path to company with divinity.'

May Master Gotama teach me that path to company with divinity."

"Then listen, student, and attend carefully. I will speak."

"Yes, Master Gotama," Subha replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Four Divine Abidings

"And what, student, is the path to company with divinity?

Here, student, a bhikkhu remains pervading one direction with a heart
accompanied by friendliness. So too the second, the third, and the fourth.
In the same way above, below, across, everywhere, and in every respect, he
remains pervading the whole world with a heart accompanied by friendliness,
abundant, expansive, vast, immeasurable, free of enmity, and free of ill will.

When the liberation of mind through friendliness has been developed in this
way, any deed measured out in a limited way does not remain there and does not
persist there.

Suppose there were a strong conch-blower who could make himself heard in the
four directions without difficulty. In the same way, when the liberation of
mind through friendliness has been developed in this way, any deed measured
out in a limited way does not remain there and does not persist there.

This too is a path to company with divinity.

Again, a bhikkhu remains pervading one direction with a heart accompanied by
kindness towards others. So too the second, the third, and the fourth. In the
same way above, below, across, everywhere, and in every respect, he remains
pervading the whole world with a heart accompanied by kindness towards others,
abundant, expansive, vast, immeasurable, free of enmity, and free of ill
will.

When the liberation of mind through kindness towards others has been developed
in this way, any deed measured out in a limited way does not remain there and
does not persist there.

Suppose there were a strong conch-blower who could make himself heard in the
four directions without difficulty. In the same way, when the liberation of
mind through kindness towards others has been developed in this way, any deed
measured out in a limited way does not remain there and does not persist
there.

This too is a path to company with divinity.

Again, a bhikkhu remains pervading one direction with a heart accompanied by
gladness. So too the second, the third, and the fourth. In the same way above,
below, across, everywhere, and in every respect, he remains pervading the
whole world with a heart accompanied by gladness, abundant, expansive, vast,
immeasurable, free of enmity, and free of ill will.

When the liberation of mind through gladness has been developed in this way,
any deed measured out in a limited way does not remain there and does not
persist there.

Suppose there were a strong conch-blower who could make himself heard in the
four directions without difficulty. In the same way, when the liberation of
mind through gladness has been developed in this way, any deed measured out in
a limited way does not remain there and does not persist there.

This too is a path to company with divinity.

Again, a bhikkhu remains pervading one direction with a heart accompanied by
dynamic balance. So too the second, the third, and the fourth. In the same
way above, below, across, everywhere, and in every respect, he remains
pervading the whole world with a heart accompanied by dynamic balance,
abundant, expansive, vast, immeasurable, free of enmity, and free of ill
will.

When the liberation of mind through dynamic balance has been developed in this
way, any deed measured out in a limited way does not remain there and does not
persist there.

Suppose there were a strong conch-blower who could make himself heard in the
four directions without difficulty. In the same way, when the liberation of
mind through dynamic balance has been developed in this way, any deed measured
out in a limited way does not remain there and does not persist there.

This too is a path to company with divinity."

### Refuge

When this was said, Subha said to the Buddha:

"Excellent, Master Gotama! Excellent, Master Gotama!

Just as if someone were to set upright what had been overturned, reveal what
was hidden, point out the way to someone who was lost, or hold up an oil lamp
in the dark so that those with eyes could see forms, so too Master Gotama has
made the dhamma clear in many ways.

I go for refuge to Master Gotama, to the dhamma, and to the Saṅgha of
bhikkhus. Let Master Gotama remember me as a lay follower who has gone for
refuge from this day for life.

Now, Master Gotama, we will go. We have many duties and much to do."

"Then go, student, when you think it is time."

Then Subha, Todeyya's son, delighted in and approved of what the Buddha had
said, got up from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, kept him on his right, and
left.

### Jānussoṇi's Praise

At that time the brahmin Jānussoṇi was driving out of Sāvatthī in the middle
of the day in an all-white chariot drawn by white mares. He saw Subha,
Todeyya's son, coming in the distance and said:

"Well then, where is Master Bhāradvāja coming from in the middle of the day?"

"Good man, I have just come from the presence of the ascetic Gotama."

"What do you think, Master Bhāradvāja? Do you consider the ascetic Gotama
wise in his clarity of understanding?"

"Good man, who am I to know the clarity of understanding of the ascetic
Gotama? One would have to be like him to know the clarity of his
understanding."

"Master Bhāradvāja praises the ascetic Gotama with high praise indeed."

"Good man, who am I to praise the ascetic Gotama? He is praised by those who
are praised as the best among gods and humans.

And those five dhammas that the brahmins lay down for making merit and for
succeeding in what is skillful, the ascetic Gotama says are supports of the
mind for developing a mind free of enmity and ill will."

When this was said, Jānussoṇi got down from his all-white chariot, arranged
his upper robe over one shoulder, extended his joined palms toward the Buddha,
and exclaimed:

"Lucky is King Pasenadi of Kosala, very lucky is King Pasenadi of Kosala, in
whose realm the Tathāgata lives, the arahant, the rightly self-awakened
Buddha!"
