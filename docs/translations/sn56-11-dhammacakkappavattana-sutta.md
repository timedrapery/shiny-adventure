## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Bārāṇasī, at Isipatana, in the Deer
Park.

There the Buddha addressed the group of five bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus."

"Bhante," they replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Two Extremes

"Bhikkhus, one gone forth should not go near these two extremes. Which two?

Devotion to the pleasure of sensuality: low, vulgar, ordinary, ignoble, and
unbeneficial.

Devotion to self-mortification: painful, ignoble, and unbeneficial.

Without going near either extreme, the Tathāgata has awakened to the middle
path of practice, which gives vision, gives knowledge, and leads to peace,
higher knowing, awakening, and nibbāna.

And what is that middle path of practice awakened to by the Tathāgata, which
gives vision, gives knowledge, and leads to peace, higher knowing, awakening,
and nibbāna?

It is just this noble eightfold path:

- right view
- right attitude
- right speech
- right action
- right livelihood
- right effort
- right remembering
- right mental composure

This is the middle path of practice awakened to by the Tathāgata, which gives
vision, gives knowledge, and leads to peace, higher knowing, awakening, and
nibbāna.

### The Noble Truth of Dissatisfaction

"And this, bhikkhus, is the noble truth of dissatisfaction:

birth is dissatisfaction;
ageing is dissatisfaction;
illness is dissatisfaction;
death is dissatisfaction;
sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and despair are dissatisfaction;
association with what is disliked is dissatisfaction;
separation from what is liked is dissatisfaction;
not getting what one wants is dissatisfaction;
in brief, the five clung-to heaps are dissatisfaction.

### The Noble Truth of the Origin of Dissatisfaction

"And this, bhikkhus, is the noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction:

that ignorant wanting that leads to renewed becoming, accompanied by relishing
and passion, delighting here and there. That is:

- ignorant wanting for sensuality
- ignorant wanting for becoming
- ignorant wanting for non-becoming

### The Noble Truth of the Quenching of Dissatisfaction

"And this, bhikkhus, is the noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction:

the complete fading and quenching of that very ignorant wanting: giving it up,
relinquishing it, freedom, non-attachment.

### The Noble Truth of the Path

"And this, bhikkhus, is the noble truth of the path of practice leading to the
quenching of dissatisfaction:

it is just this noble eightfold path:

- right view
- right attitude
- right speech
- right action
- right livelihood
- right effort
- right remembering
- right mental composure

### The Three Turnings

"'This is the noble truth of dissatisfaction.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of dissatisfaction should be fully understood.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of dissatisfaction has been fully understood.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This is the noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction should be abandoned.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the origin of dissatisfaction has been abandoned.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This is the noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction should be directly
known.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the quenching of dissatisfaction has been directly known.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This is the noble truth of the path of practice leading to the quenching of
dissatisfaction.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the path of practice leading to the quenching of
dissatisfaction should be developed.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

"'This noble truth of the path of practice leading to the quenching of
dissatisfaction has been developed.'

Vision arose in me, knowledge arose in me, discernment arose in me, clear
knowledge arose in me, and light arose in me regarding things not heard before.

### The Buddha's Declaration

"As long as this three-round, twelve-aspect knowledge and seeing regarding
these four noble truths as they really are was not fully purified, I did not
claim unsurpassed full awakening in the world with its devas, Māras, and
Brahmās, in this generation with its samaṇas and brahmins, its rulers and its
people.

But when this three-round, twelve-aspect knowledge and seeing regarding these
four noble truths as they really are was fully purified, then I did claim
unsurpassed full awakening in the world with its devas, Māras, and Brahmās, in
this generation with its samaṇas and brahmins, its rulers and its people.

Knowledge and seeing arose in me:

'My release is unshakeable.
This is the last birth.
Now there is no more renewed becoming.'"

### Kondañña's Dhamma Eye

That is what the Buddha said. The group of five bhikkhus were glad and welcomed
what the Buddha had said.

While this explanation was being given, the dustless, stainless Dhamma-eye
arose in Venerable Kondañña:

"Whatever is subject to origin is all subject to quenching."

### The Wheel Rolls

When the Blessed One had set the wheel of Dhamma rolling, the earth devas cried
out:

"Near Bārāṇasī, at Isipatana in the Deer Park, the Blessed One has set rolling
the unsurpassed wheel of Dhamma that cannot be rolled back by any samaṇa or
brahmin or deva or Māra or Brahmā or anyone in the world."

On hearing the cry of the earth devas, the devas of the Four Great Kings took
up the cry; then the devas of the Thirty-three, the Yāma devas, the Contented
devas, the devas delighting in creation, the devas wielding power over others'
creations, and the Brahmā-company devas took up the same cry:

"Near Bārāṇasī, at Isipatana in the Deer Park, the Blessed One has set rolling
the unsurpassed wheel of Dhamma that cannot be rolled back by any samaṇa or
brahmin or deva or Māra or Brahmā or anyone in the world."

At that moment the cry rose all the way to the Brahmā worlds. This ten-thousand
world system shook, trembled, and quivered, and a vast immeasurable radiance
appeared in the world, surpassing the glory of the devas.

Then the Blessed One exclaimed:

"Kondañña really knows. Kondañña really knows."

That is how Venerable Kondañña came to be known as Aññā-Kondañña.
