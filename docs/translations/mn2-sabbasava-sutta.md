# MN 2: Sabbāsava Sutta (All the Outflows)

## Editorial Note

- This is a repo-native study translation of MN 2, designed to be readable as
  a single Markdown document.
- Companion translator notes:
  [MN 2 Translation Notes](mn2-sabbasava-sutta-notes.md)
- Source basis: the Pali text of MN 2 as segmented in SuttaCentral's Bilara
  data.
- House choices that most affect this sutta: `āsava` is rendered `outflow`;
  `āsavānaṃ khayā` is rendered `through the wearing away of the outflows`;
  `yoniso manasikāra` is rendered `wise attention`; `ayoniso manasikāra` is
  rendered `unwise attention`; `pahāna` is rendered `abandoning`; `saṃvara` is
  rendered `restraint`; `paṭisevanā` is rendered `using`; `adhivāsanā` is
  rendered `enduring`; `parivajjanā` is rendered `avoiding`; `vinodanā` is
  rendered `dispelling`; and `bhāvanā` is rendered `development`.
- This edition treats MN 2 as the control passage for the seven outflow methods:
  seeing, restraint, using, enduring, avoiding, dispelling, and developing.
- This is the first stable study edition of the repository's MN 2 translation
  surface.

## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Sāvatthī, in Jeta's Grove, in
Anāthapiṇḍika's monastery.

There the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus."

"Bhante," they replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Method for All Outflows

"Bhikkhus, I will teach you the method for restraining all outflows. Listen
carefully, attend well, and I will speak."

"Yes, bhante," the bhikkhus replied.

The Buddha said this:

"Bhikkhus, I say there is the wearing away of the outflows for one who knows
and sees, not for one who does not know and does not see.

And what does one know and see, so that I say there is the wearing away of the
outflows?

Wise attention and unwise attention.

For one who attends unwisely, unarisen outflows arise and arisen outflows grow.

For one who attends wisely, unarisen outflows do not arise and arisen outflows
are abandoned.

There are outflows to be abandoned by seeing.
There are outflows to be abandoned by restraint.
There are outflows to be abandoned by using.
There are outflows to be abandoned by enduring.
There are outflows to be abandoned by avoiding.
There are outflows to be abandoned by dispelling.
There are outflows to be abandoned by developing.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Seeing

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by seeing?

Here, bhikkhus, an unlearned ordinary person has not seen the noble ones, is
unskilled in the noble Dhamma, and is untrained in the noble Dhamma. He has not
seen good persons, is unskilled in the good person's Dhamma, and is untrained
in the good person's Dhamma.

He does not discern which qualities should be attended to, and he does not
discern which qualities should not be attended to.

Not discerning which qualities should be attended to and which should not be
attended to, he attends to qualities that should not be attended to, and does
not attend to qualities that should be attended to.

And what are the qualities that should not be attended to, which he attends to?

They are qualities such that, when he attends to them:

- an unarisen sensual outflow arises, or an arisen sensual outflow grows
- an unarisen outflow of becoming arises, or an arisen outflow of becoming grows
- an unarisen outflow of ignorance arises, or an arisen outflow of ignorance grows

These are the qualities that should not be attended to, which he attends to.

And what are the qualities that should be attended to, which he does not attend
to?

They are qualities such that, when he attends to them:

- an unarisen sensual outflow does not arise, or an arisen sensual outflow is
  abandoned
- an unarisen outflow of becoming does not arise, or an arisen outflow of
  becoming is abandoned
- an unarisen outflow of ignorance does not arise, or an arisen outflow of
  ignorance is abandoned

These are the qualities that should be attended to, which he does not attend
to.

Because he attends to what should not be attended to, and does not attend to
what should be attended to, unarisen outflows arise and arisen outflows grow.

### Unwise Questions

He attends unwisely in this way:

"Was I in the past?
Was I not in the past?
What was I in the past?
How was I in the past?
Having been what, what was I in the past?

Will I be in the future?
Will I not be in the future?
What will I be in the future?
How will I be in the future?
Having been what, what will I be in the future?"

Or else, in the present, he is inwardly uncertain:

"Am I?
Am I not?
What am I?
How am I?
Where has this being come from?
Where will it go?"

### The Thicket of Views

When he attends unwisely in this way, one of six views arises in him.

The view "I have a self" arises in him as true and established.

Or the view "I have no self" arises in him as true and established.

Or the view "I recognize self by self" arises in him as true and established.

Or the view "I recognize not-self by self" arises in him as true and
established.

Or the view "I recognize self by not-self" arises in him as true and
established.

Or else he has this view:

"This self of mine, which speaks and feels and experiences the result of good
and bad actions here and there, is permanent, lasting, eternal, not subject to
change, and will stand forever."

This, bhikkhus, is called a view-position, a view-thicket, a view-wilderness, a
view-distortion, a view-wriggling, and a fetter of views.

Bound by the fetter of views, bhikkhus, the unlearned ordinary person is not
freed from birth, ageing, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, distress, and
despair.

I say he is not freed from dissatisfaction.

### Wise Attention

But, bhikkhus, a learned noble disciple has seen the noble ones, is skilled in
the noble Dhamma, and is well trained in the noble Dhamma. He has seen good
persons, is skilled in the good person's Dhamma, and is well trained in the
good person's Dhamma.

He discerns which qualities should be attended to, and he discerns which
qualities should not be attended to.

Discerning which qualities should be attended to and which should not be
attended to, he does not attend to qualities that should not be attended to, and
he attends to qualities that should be attended to.

And what are the qualities that should not be attended to, which he does not
attend to?

They are qualities such that, when he attends to them:

- an unarisen sensual outflow arises, or an arisen sensual outflow grows
- an unarisen outflow of becoming arises, or an arisen outflow of becoming grows
- an unarisen outflow of ignorance arises, or an arisen outflow of ignorance grows

These are the qualities that should not be attended to, which he does not
attend to.

And what are the qualities that should be attended to, which he attends to?

They are qualities such that, when he attends to them:

- an unarisen sensual outflow does not arise, or an arisen sensual outflow is
  abandoned
- an unarisen outflow of becoming does not arise, or an arisen outflow of
  becoming is abandoned
- an unarisen outflow of ignorance does not arise, or an arisen outflow of
  ignorance is abandoned

These are the qualities that should be attended to, which he attends to.

Because he does not attend to what should not be attended to, and attends to
what should be attended to, unarisen outflows do not arise and arisen outflows
are abandoned.

He wisely attends:

"This is dissatisfaction."
"This is the origin of dissatisfaction."
"This is the quenching of dissatisfaction."
"This is the path of practice leading to the quenching of dissatisfaction."

When he attends wisely in this way, three fetters are abandoned:

- identity view
- uncertain distraction
- grasping at rules and observances

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by seeing.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Restraint

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by restraint?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu lives restrained by restraint of
the eye faculty.

If he lived unrestrained in the eye faculty, outflows, distress, and fever
might arise. When he lives restrained in the eye faculty, those outflows,
distress, and fever do not arise.

Reflecting wisely, he lives restrained by restraint of the ear faculty.

Reflecting wisely, he lives restrained by restraint of the nose faculty.

Reflecting wisely, he lives restrained by restraint of the tongue faculty.

Reflecting wisely, he lives restrained by restraint of the body faculty.

Reflecting wisely, he lives restrained by restraint of the thinking-mind
faculty.

If he lived unrestrained in the thinking-mind faculty, outflows, distress, and
fever might arise. When he lives restrained in the thinking-mind faculty, those
outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

If he lived unrestrained in restraint, outflows, distress, and fever might
arise. When he lives restrained in restraint, those outflows, distress, and
fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by restraint.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Using

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by using?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu uses a robe only for warding off
cold, warding off heat, warding off the contact of flies, mosquitoes, wind,
sun, and reptiles, and only for covering the private parts.

Reflecting wisely, he uses almsfood not for amusement, not for intoxication,
not for adornment, and not for beautification, but only for the maintenance and
continuance of this body, for ending harm, and for supporting the spiritual
life, thinking:

"In this way I will put down old felt experience and not give rise to new felt
experience. I will have maintenance, blamelessness, and easeful living."

Reflecting wisely, he uses lodging only for warding off cold, warding off heat,
warding off the contact of flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and reptiles, and only
for dispelling weather dangers and enjoying retreat.

Reflecting wisely, he uses medicines and supplies for the sick only for
countering arisen painful illness-related felt experiences, and for the highest
freedom from affliction.

If he did not use these things, outflows, distress, and fever might arise. When
he uses them, those outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by using.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Enduring

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by enduring?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu endures cold and heat, hunger and
thirst, and the contact of flies, mosquitoes, wind, sun, and reptiles. He
endures rude and unwelcome speech. He is the kind of person who endures arisen
bodily felt experiences that are painful, sharp, severe, bitter, unpleasant,
disagreeable, and life-threatening.

If he did not endure them, outflows, distress, and fever might arise. When he
endures them, those outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by enduring.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Avoiding

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by avoiding?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu avoids a dangerous elephant, a
dangerous horse, a dangerous ox, a dangerous dog, a snake, a stump, a thorny
place, a pit, a cliff, a cesspit, and a sewer.

Reflecting wisely, he avoids an unsuitable seat, an unsuitable resort, and bad
friends, where knowledgeable companions in the spiritual life might suspect one
of bad places.

If he did not avoid these things, outflows, distress, and fever might arise.
When he avoids them, those outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by avoiding.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Dispelling

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by dispelling?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu does not endure an arisen thinking
of sensuality. He abandons it, dispels it, ends it, and brings it to non-being.

He does not endure an arisen thinking of ill will. He abandons it, dispels it,
ends it, and brings it to non-being.

He does not endure an arisen thinking of harming. He abandons it, dispels it,
ends it, and brings it to non-being.

He does not endure any evil, unwholesome qualities as they arise. He abandons
them, dispels them, ends them, and brings them to non-being.

If he did not dispel them, outflows, distress, and fever might arise. When he
dispels them, those outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by dispelling.

### Outflows to Be Abandoned by Developing

And what, bhikkhus, are the outflows to be abandoned by developing?

Here, bhikkhus, reflecting wisely, a bhikkhu develops the remembering awakening
factor, based on seclusion, based on fading, based on quenching, maturing in
relinquishment.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the discernment-of-qualities awakening factor,
based on seclusion, based on fading, based on quenching, maturing in
relinquishment.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the energy awakening factor.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the delight awakening factor.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the relaxation awakening factor.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the mental-composure awakening factor.

Reflecting wisely, he develops the dynamic-balance awakening factor, based on
seclusion, based on fading, based on quenching, maturing in relinquishment.

If he did not develop them, outflows, distress, and fever might arise. When he
develops them, those outflows, distress, and fever do not arise.

These are called the outflows to be abandoned by developing.

### Fully Restrained

Bhikkhus, when for a bhikkhu the outflows to be abandoned by seeing have been
abandoned by seeing; the outflows to be abandoned by restraint have been
abandoned by restraint; the outflows to be abandoned by using have been
abandoned by using; the outflows to be abandoned by enduring have been
abandoned by enduring; the outflows to be abandoned by avoiding have been
abandoned by avoiding; the outflows to be abandoned by dispelling have been
abandoned by dispelling; and the outflows to be abandoned by developing have
been abandoned by developing, this is called a bhikkhu who lives restrained by
the restraint of all outflows.

He has cut off ignorant wanting, undone the fetter, and by rightly
comprehending conceit has made an end of dissatisfaction."

That is what the Buddha said.

The bhikkhus were satisfied and welcomed what the Buddha had said.

The Sabbāsava Sutta, the second discourse, is finished.
