# AN 11.9 Translation Notes

This document is the companion rationale for
[AN 11.9: Saddha Sutta](an11-9-saddha-sutta.md). The main translation is
the primary study surface; this file records debated translation choices,
source-audit calls, and edition notes that govern it.

## Source Base

- Primary source: the Pali text of AN 11.9 as segmented in SuttaCentral's
  Bilara data.
- Control surface: the repository's current term policy, especially
  `jhayati`, `jhana`, `nivarana`, `citta`, `mano`, and `sanna`.
- Working method: the discourse was governed as the second Aṅguttara Nikāya
  translation surface, treating the `jhāyati` verb as the surface's central
  editorial problem — the same verb covers both the wild colt's
  fodder-obsession and the thoroughbred's formless thinking, and the
  translation must preserve that dual application without collapsing it.

## Governing Decisions

- `jhāyati` is rendered as two distinct English forms depending on
  application. In the instruction and positive contexts — the opening
  imperative, the thoroughbred's conclusion `jhāyati ca pana`, and the
  explanatory restatements — it is rendered `think like this`, following the
  house default for `jhāyati` (major, reviewed). In the pejorative
  descriptive passages — the wild colt holding a theme of fodder, and the
  eleven-bases list — it is rendered `holds as theme`, keeping the
  `jhāna` → `mental theme` root policy audible while avoiding the awkwardness
  of "thinks like this: fodder." The two forms are documented here rather
  than in a new term record because the split is local to this surface's
  dual-voice structure.
- `jhāyati pajjhāyati nijjhāyati avajjhāyati` is rendered `think on it,
  dwell on it, brood over it, and keep circling back to it`. The four verbs
  (simple + pa- + ni- + ava- prefixed forms of the same root) show escalating
  fixation on the distraction as theme. The rendering keeps each step
  distinct without importing clinical psychology vocabulary.
- `citta` in `pariyuṭṭhitena cetasā viharati` is rendered `feeling mind`,
  the house default. `Pariyuṭṭhita` (overcome, beset) is rendered `overcome`;
  `pareta` (seized, overpowered) is rendered `seized`. The phrase
  `kāmarāgapariyuṭṭhitena cetasā viharati kāmarāgaparetena` becomes `remain
  with their feeling mind overcome and seized by sensual distraction`. The
  double adjective (overcome + seized) appears only for the first and fifth
  distractions (kāmacchanda and vicikicchā); the middle three use only
  `overcome`.
- `saññā vibhūtā hoti` is rendered `recognition has vanished`. The house
  term for `saññā` is `recognition`. `Vibhūtā` (past participle) is rendered
  `has vanished` — the recognition of each basis has disappeared, which is
  why the thoroughbred holds none of them as theme. This is the sutta's
  central explanatory formula and the heading of the final section.
- `mano` in `anuvicaritaṁ manasā` is rendered `thinking mind`, the house
  default for `mano`. This keeps `mano` distinct from `citta` (`feeling
  mind`) throughout the eleven-bases list.

## Existing Control Records Reused

AN 11.9 does not need new live phrase records. The governing terms already
have sufficient lexicon coverage:

- `jhāyati` → `think like this` (major, reviewed)
- `jhāna` → `mental theme` (major, reviewed)
- `nīvaraṇa` members → governed distraction-family renderings (major)
- `citta` → `feeling mind` (major)
- `mano` → `thinking mind` (major)
- `saññā` → `recognition` (major)

## Re-audit Calls

- `ājānīyajhāyitaṁ … jhāya; mā khaḷuṅkajhāyitaṁ` is rendered `think like
  a thoroughbred — not in the way of a wild colt`. The gerundive compound
  nouns (thoroughbred-thinking, wild-colt-thinking) are translated as
  comparative phrases rather than abstract action nouns, which keeps the
  English instruction crisp.
- `yavasaṁ yavasan` (fodder, fodder) is kept as `Fodder, fodder!` — the
  Pali reduplication shows obsessive fixation on a single object. The
  exclamation mark is an editorial choice to make the obsessive quality
  audible in English.
- `patodassa ajjhoharaṇaṁ` is rendered `goad's contact`. The compound
  `ajjhoharaṇa` (entering, application, bringing-down-onto) is treated as
  the moment the goad touches the horse — the contact event the thoroughbred
  dreads even as a debt. `Goad's contact` keeps the link to `patoda` visible
  without importing later technical impact vocabulary.
- `iṇaṁ … bandhaṁ … jāniṁ … kaliṁ` is rendered `a debt, a bond, a loss,
  and a misfortune`. These four nouns are the fine thoroughbred's view of
  what the goad's contact means — escalating from financial metaphor (debt,
  bond) to outcome metaphor (loss, misfortune).
- `saindā devā sabrahmakā sapajāpatikā ārakāva namassanti` is rendered `the
  gods together with Indra, Brahmā, and Pajāpati worship them from afar`.
  The three sa- compounds name the divine assembly; `ārakāva` (from afar, at
  a distance) is kept visible as `from afar` because the distance is the
  teaching point — even the gods cannot identify the thoroughbred's basis.
- The verse `Namo te purisājañña, / namo te purisuttama; / Yassa te
  nābhijānāma, / yampi nissāya jhāyasī` is rendered with `purisājañña`
  (thoroughbred individual) and `purisuttama` (supreme among persons) keeping
  the house term `individual` for the purisa family. The final couplet
  `yassa te nābhijānāma, yampi nissāya jhāyasī` — "we do not know that of
  you, the basis on which you think like this" — is rendered `We do not
  understand / on what your thinking stands`, which preserves the `nissāya`
  (basis, dependence) without requiring the reader to hold the full eleven-
  item list in mind.
- The sutta's name in the Bilara root text is `Saddhasutta` (Saddha =
  faith/confidence used as a personal name). Some editions and translators
  use `Sandha`. This surface follows the Bilara root text: the bhikkhu is
  addressed as `Saddha` throughout.
- The `…pe…` abbreviation marks in the Bilara (segments 2.11–2.13 and
  3.9–3.11, and the restatement in 7.2) are expanded in full throughout the
  translation, consistent with the repository's policy of preserving
  repetition for study readability.
- `nevasaññānāsaññāyatana` is rendered `the base of
  neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition`, keeping `saññā` aligned with
  its house default `recognition` throughout the eleven-base list and the
  explanation.

## Editorial Presentation

- The main translation is divided into five sections: `The Setting`, `The
  Wild Colt's Way`, `The Fine Thoroughbred's Way`, `Saddha's Question`, and
  `Recognition Has Vanished`. The last heading names the key explanatory
  formula `saññā vibhūtā` directly.
- The wild colt and thoroughbred sections each present the horse analogy
  first, then the parallel human application — following the Pali structure
  exactly.
- Saddha's question restates the full eleven-base list as a question, which
  the Pali also does. Repetition is preserved rather than collapsed because
  the restatement is part of the dialogue's teaching dynamic, not filler.

## Edition Status

- This companion supports the first stable study edition of the repo's
  AN 11.9 translation surface.
- Future revisions should focus on the `jhāyati` dual-rendering decision if
  the jhāna cluster later establishes a single verb policy that covers both
  the obsessive and the positive applications, or the `saññā vibhūtā`
  formula if the sanna record develops a narrower governing phrase.
