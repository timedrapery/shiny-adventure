# MN 18: Madhupiṇḍika Sutta (Honey Ball)

## Editorial Note

  `phassa` is rendered `contact`; `vedanā` is rendered `felt experience`;
  `saññā` is rendered `recognition`; `sañjānāti` is rendered `recognizes`;
  `vitakka` is rendered `thinking`; `papañca` is rendered `proliferation`;
  `papañcasaññāsaṅkhā` is rendered `recognitions and notions of

  untranslated where the English would narrow it too fast.

## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying among the Sakyans at Kapilavatthu, in the
Nigrodha grove.

Then, in the morning, the Buddha robed up and, taking bowl and robe, entered
Kapilavatthu for alms.

After going for alms in Kapilavatthu, and after returning from the meal, he
went to the Great Wood for the day, plunged deep into the Great Wood, and sat
down at the root of a beluva sapling.

### Daṇḍapāṇi's Question

Then Daṇḍapāṇi the Sakyan, while walking and strolling, went to the Great
Wood, plunged deep into it, approached the Buddha, and exchanged greetings
with him.

When the greetings and courteous talk were concluded, he stood to one side,
leaning on his staff, and said to the Buddha:

"What does the ascetic say? What does he declare?"

"Friend, I speak in such a way that one does not stand quarreling with anyone
in the world with its gods, Māras, and Brahmās, among this population with its
ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans. And I speak in such a way that
recognitions do not underlie that brahmin living detached from
sensuality, free from doubt, with uncertainty cut off, with ignorant wanting
worn away in every kind of becoming.

That is what I say, friend; that is what I declare."

When this was said, Daṇḍapāṇi the Sakyan shook his head, stuck out his tongue,
raised three furrows on his forehead, and left leaning on his staff.

### The Bhikkhus Ask What He Meant

Then, in the late afternoon, the Buddha came out of seclusion, went to the
Nigrodha grove, sat down on the seat prepared, and told the bhikkhus about the
exchange with Daṇḍapāṇi.

Then one bhikkhu said to him:

"But, bhante, speaking in what way does the Buddha not stand quarreling with
anyone in the world with its gods, Māras, and Brahmās, among this population
with its ascetics and brahmins, its gods and humans?

And how is it that recognitions do not underlie the Buddha, that brahmin living
detached from sensuality, free from doubt, with uncertainty cut off,
with ignorant wanting worn away in every kind of becoming?"

### The Buddha's Summary

"Bhikkhu, from the source by which the recognitions and notions of
proliferation sweep over a person:

if there is nothing there to delight in, to affirm, or to take personally,
this is the end of the underlying tendency to passion, the end of the
underlying tendency to aversion, the end of the underlying tendency to views,
the end of the underlying tendency to doubt, the end of the underlying
tendency to conceit, the end of the underlying tendency to passion for
becoming, the end of the underlying tendency to ignorance, the end of taking
up rods and blades, the end of quarrels, disputes, arguments, contention,
slander, and false speech.

Here these bad unwholesome dhammas cease without remainder."

That is what the Buddha said. Then the Fortunate One got up from his seat and
entered his dwelling.

### Mahākaccāna Is Asked to Explain

Soon after the Buddha had gone, the bhikkhus thought:

"The Buddha gave a brief summary and entered his dwelling without explaining
the meaning in detail. Who could explain the detailed meaning of this brief
summary?"

Then they thought:

"This venerable Mahākaccāna is praised by the Buddha and esteemed by wise
fellow practitioners. He is capable of explaining the detailed meaning of this
brief summary. Let us go to him and ask him."

So they went to venerable Mahākaccāna, exchanged greetings with him, sat down
to one side, and asked him to explain the matter.

Mahākaccāna first said:

"Friends, it is as though a person in need of heartwood, seeking heartwood,
would pass by the root and trunk of a great standing tree full of heartwood
and imagine that heartwood should be sought among the branches and leaves.

That is what has happened here. While the Teacher himself was present face to
face, you passed him by and thought this matter should be asked of me.

Still, listen, friends, and attend carefully. I will speak."

"Yes, friend," the bhikkhus replied.

### The Sequence Through the Six Sense Doors

Mahākaccāna said:

"Friends, the Buddha gave this brief summary and then entered his dwelling
without explaining the meaning in detail:

'From the source by which the recognitions and notions of proliferation sweep
over a person ... here these bad unwholesome dhammas cease without remainder.'

I understand the detailed meaning of that brief summary like this:

Dependent on eye and visible forms, eye-knowing arises. The meeting of the three is
contact. With contact as condition, felt experience. What one feels, one
recognizes. What one recognizes, one thinks about. What one thinks about, one
proliferates about. From what one proliferates about as the source, the
recognitions and notions of proliferation sweep over a person when it comes to
visible forms knowable by the eye in the past, future, and present.

The same pattern applies for ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and
tastes, and body and tangibles.

Dependent on mind and dhammas, mind-knowing arises. The meeting of the three
is contact. With contact as condition, felt experience. What one feels, one
recognizes. What one recognizes, one thinks about. What one thinks about, one
proliferates about. From what one proliferates about as the source, the
recognitions and notions of proliferation sweep over a person when it comes to
dhammas knowable by the mind in the past, future, and present."

### When the Process Can Be Designated

"Friends, where there are eye, visible forms, and eye-knowing, it is possible to
designate contact.

Where contact can be designated, it is possible to designate felt experience.

Where felt experience can be designated, it is possible to designate
recognition.

Where recognition can be designated, it is possible to designate thinking.

Where thinking can be designated, it is possible to designate the sweeping-over
of the recognitions and notions of proliferation.

The same pattern applies for ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and
tastes, and body and tangibles.

Where there are mind, dhammas, and mind-knowing, it is possible to designate
contact.

Where contact can be designated, it is possible to designate felt experience.

Where felt experience can be designated, it is possible to designate
recognition.

Where recognition can be designated, it is possible to designate thinking.

Where thinking can be designated, it is possible to designate the sweeping-over
of the recognitions and notions of proliferation."

### When the Process Cannot Be Designated

"Friends, where there are no eye, no visible forms, and no eye-knowing, it is not
possible to designate contact.

Where contact cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate felt
experience.

Where felt experience cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate
recognition.

Where recognition cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate
thinking.

Where thinking cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate the
sweeping-over of the recognitions and notions of proliferation.

The same pattern applies for ear and sounds, nose and smells, tongue and
tastes, and body and tangibles.

Where there are no mind, no dhammas, and no mind-knowing, it is not possible
to designate contact.

Where contact cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate felt
experience.

Where felt experience cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate
recognition.

Where recognition cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate
thinking.

Where thinking cannot be designated, it is not possible to designate the
sweeping-over of the recognitions and notions of proliferation.

That is how I understand the detailed meaning of the brief summary given by
the Buddha.

If you wish, friends, you may go to the Buddha and ask him about this matter.
Remember it as the Buddha explains it."

### The Buddha Confirms Mahākaccāna

Then the bhikkhus, delighted with what Mahākaccāna had said, went to the
Buddha, bowed, sat down to one side, and told him the whole exchange,
concluding:

"Mahākaccāna explained the meaning to us in this way, with these words and
these phrases."

"Bhikkhus, Mahākaccāna is wise; Mahākaccāna has great discernment.

If you had asked me about this matter, I too would have explained it in just
the same way as Mahākaccāna explained it.

That is what it means. That is how you should remember it."

### The Honey Ball

When this was said, venerable Ānanda said to the Buddha:

"Bhante, it is as though a person weak with hunger were to obtain a honey
ball. Wherever one might taste it, one would get only a sweet, delicious
flavor.

In the same way, wherever a capable bhikkhu were to examine with discernment
the meaning of this dhamma exposition, one would get only gladness and
clarity of mind.

What is the name of this dhamma exposition, bhante?"

"Then, Ānanda, remember this dhamma exposition as The Honey Ball."

That is what the Buddha said. Venerable Ānanda was glad and welcomed what the
Buddha had said.
