# MN 1: Mūlapariyāya Sutta (The Root Sequence)

## Editorial Note

- This is a repo-native study translation of MN 1, designed to be readable
  as a single Markdown document.
- Companion translator notes:
  [MN 1 Translation Notes](mn1-mulapariyaya-sutta-notes.md)
- Source basis: the Pali text of MN 1 in the SuttaCentral / Bilara tradition.
- House choices that most affect this sutta: `bhikkhu` remains untranslated;
  `puthujjana` is rendered `ordinary person`; `sekha` is rendered `trainee`;
  `sañjānāti` is rendered `recognizes`; the `pariññā` family is rendered with
  `full understanding`; `nibbāna` remains untranslated; and `Tathāgata`,
  `arahant`, and `buddha` remain untranslated as technical titles.
- This is the first stable study edition of the repository's MN 1
  translation surface.

## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was dwelling at Ukkaṭṭhā, in the Subhaga grove, at the
root of a royal sāla tree.

There the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus."

"Venerable sir," they replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Root Sequence

"Bhikkhus, I will teach you the root sequence of all things. Listen carefully,
attend well, and I will speak."

"Yes, venerable sir," the bhikkhus replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Ordinary Person

ones and is untrained and undisciplined in their Dhamma, who has no regard for
good people and is untrained and undisciplined in their Dhamma:

The repeated pattern shows how recognition is followed by selfing, ownership,
and delight, whether the object is coarse, subtle, meditative, totalizing, or
even nibbāna-language itself.*

in play, so the wording remains prohibitive rather than completed.*

### The Arahant: Fully Understood

"Bhikkhus, a bhikkhu who is an arahant, whose outflows are worn away, who has
lived the spiritual life, done what had to be done, laid down the burden,
reached the goal, worn away the fetters of becoming, and is rightly released
through final knowledge:

- One directly knows earth as earth. Having directly known earth as earth, one
  does not take oneself to be earth, does not take oneself in earth, does not
  take oneself apart from earth, does not take earth as 'mine', and does not
  delight in earth. Why is that? Because it has been fully understood, I say.
- The same pattern holds for water, fire, air, beings, gods, Pajāpati,
  Brahmā, Abhassara, Subhakiṇha, Vehapphala, Abhibhū, the sphere of boundless
  space, the sphere of boundless knowing, the sphere of nothingness, the
  sphere of neither-recognition-nor-non-recognition, the seen, the heard, the
  sensed, the cognized, oneness, diversity, all, and nibbāna: the arahant
  directly knows each as it is named, does not take oneself to be it, in it,
  or apart from it, does not take it as 'mine', and does not delight in it.
  Why is that? Because it has been fully understood, I say.

### The Arahant: Lust Worn Away

"Bhikkhus, again, a bhikkhu who is an arahant, whose outflows are worn away,
who has lived the spiritual life, done what had to be done, laid down the
burden, reached the goal, worn away the fetters of becoming, and is rightly
released through final knowledge:

- From earth through nibbāna, the same pattern applies: the arahant directly
  knows each as it is named, does not take oneself to be it, in it, or apart
  from it, does not take it as 'mine', and does not delight in it. Why is
  that? Because lust is absent through the wearing away of lust.

### The Tathāgata: Understood To The End

"Bhikkhus, the Tathāgata, the arahant, the fully awakened Buddha:

- The same pattern applies from earth through nibbāna: the Tathāgata directly
  knows each as it is named, does not take oneself to be it, in it, or apart
  from it, does not take it as 'mine', and does not delight in it. Why is
  that? Because it has been fully understood to the end by the Tathāgata, I
  say.

### The Tathāgata: Fully Awakened

"Bhikkhus, the Tathāgata, the arahant, the fully awakened Buddha:

- From earth through nibbāna, the same pattern applies: the Tathāgata
  directly knows each as it is named, does not take oneself to be it, in it,
  or apart from it, does not take it as 'mine', and does not delight in it.
- Why is that? The Tathāgata knows that delight is the root of
  dissatisfaction, that with becoming there is birth, and that for whatever is
  born there are aging and death. Therefore, with the wearing away, fading,
  quenching, giving up, and relinquishment of ignorant wanting in every way,
  the Tathāgata has awakened to unsurpassed full awakening, I say.

That is what the Buddha said. The bhikkhus did not delight in what the Buddha
had said.
