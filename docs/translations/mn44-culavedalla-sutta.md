# MN 44: Cūḷavedalla Sutta (The Shorter Analysis)

## Editorial Note

- This is a repo-native study translation of MN 44, designed to be readable as
  a single Markdown document.
- Companion translator notes:
  [MN 44 Translation Notes](mn44-culavedalla-sutta-notes.md)
- Source basis: the Pali text of MN 44 as segmented in SuttaCentral's Bilara
  data.
- House choices that most affect this sutta: `sakkāya` is rendered
  `identity`; `sakkāyadiṭṭhi` is rendered `identity view`; `upādāna` is
  rendered `taking personally`; `pañcupādānakkhandhā` is rendered `five
  clung-to heaps`; `vedanā` is rendered `felt experience`; `saññā` is rendered
  `recognition`; `viññāṇa` is rendered `knowing`; `samādhi` is rendered
  `mental composure`; `ekaggatā` is rendered `directness`; `sati` is rendered
  `remembering`; `taṇhā` is rendered `ignorant wanting`; and `nirodha` is
  rendered `quenching` except in the governed technical compound `attainment
  of cessation`.
- This edition treats MN 44 as a control passage for the relation between
  identity, the five clung-to heaps, the path factors, the three training
  heaps, mental composure, cessation attainment, felt experience, and release.
- This is the first stable study edition of the repository's MN 44 translation
  surface.

## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in the Bamboo Grove, at the
squirrels' feeding ground.

Then Visākha the lay follower went to Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī. He bowed to
her and sat to one side. Sitting there, he said to her:

### Identity

"Ayye, people say, 'identity, identity.'

What, Ayye, has the Buddha called identity?"

"Friend Visākha, the Buddha has called these five clung-to heaps identity:

- the clung-to heap of form
- the clung-to heap of felt experience
- the clung-to heap of recognition
- the clung-to heap of putting things together
- the clung-to heap of knowing

Friend Visākha, the Buddha has called these five clung-to heaps identity."

Saying, "Good, Ayye," Visākha the lay follower was glad and approved of
Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī's words. Then he asked her a further question:

### The Origin of Identity

"Ayye, people say, 'the origin of identity, the origin of identity.'

What has the Buddha called the origin of identity?"

"Friend Visākha, it is that ignorant wanting that leads to renewed becoming,
accompanied by relishing and passion, delighting here and there, that is:

- ignorant wanting for sensuality
- ignorant wanting for becoming
- ignorant wanting for non-becoming

This, friend Visākha, is what the Buddha has called the origin of identity."

### The Quenching of Identity

"Ayye, people say, 'the quenching of identity, the quenching of identity.'

What has the Buddha called the quenching of identity?"

"Friend Visākha, it is the complete fading and quenching of that very ignorant
wanting: giving it up, relinquishing it, freedom, non-attachment.

This, friend Visākha, is what the Buddha has called the quenching of identity."

### The Path to the Quenching of Identity

"Ayye, people say, 'the path of practice leading to the quenching of identity,
the path of practice leading to the quenching of identity.'

What has the Buddha called the path of practice leading to the quenching of
identity?"

"Friend Visākha, it is just this noble eightfold path:

- right view
- right attitude
- right speech
- right action
- right livelihood
- right effort
- right remembering
- right mental composure

This, friend Visākha, is what the Buddha has called the path of practice
leading to the quenching of identity."

### Taking Personally

"Ayye, is taking personally the same as those five clung-to heaps, or is
taking personally something apart from the five clung-to heaps?"

"Friend Visākha, taking personally is not the same as those five clung-to
heaps, nor is taking personally something apart from the five clung-to heaps.

The desire and passion in regard to the five clung-to heaps: that is the
taking personally there."

### Identity View

"But, Ayye, how does identity view come to be?"

"Here, friend Visākha, an unlearned ordinary person, one who has not seen the
noble ones, who is unskilled and untrained in the noble Dhamma, one who has not
seen true persons, who is unskilled and untrained in the true person's Dhamma,
regards form as self, or self as having form, or form as in self, or self as
in form.

That person regards felt experience as self, or self as having felt
experience, or felt experience as in self, or self as in felt experience.

That person regards recognition as self, or self as having recognition, or
recognition as in self, or self as in recognition.

That person regards putting things together as self, or self as having putting
things together, or putting things together as in self, or self as in putting
things together.

That person regards knowing as self, or self as having knowing, or knowing as
in self, or self as in knowing.

This, friend Visākha, is how identity view comes to be."

"But, Ayye, how does identity view not come to be?"

"Here, friend Visākha, a learned noble disciple, one who has seen the noble
ones, who is skilled and trained in the noble Dhamma, one who has seen true
persons, who is skilled and trained in the true person's Dhamma, does not
regard form as self, or self as having form, or form as in self, or self as in
form.

That disciple does not regard felt experience as self, or self as having felt
experience, or felt experience as in self, or self as in felt experience.

That disciple does not regard recognition as self, or self as having
recognition, or recognition as in self, or self as in recognition.

That disciple does not regard putting things together as self, or self as
having putting things together, or putting things together as in self, or self
as in putting things together.

That disciple does not regard knowing as self, or self as having knowing, or
knowing as in self, or self as in knowing.

This, friend Visākha, is how identity view does not come to be."

### The Noble Eightfold Path

"Ayye, what is the noble eightfold path?"

"Friend Visākha, it is just this noble eightfold path:

- right view
- right attitude
- right speech
- right action
- right livelihood
- right effort
- right remembering
- right mental composure"

"Ayye, is the noble eightfold path conditioned or unconditioned?"

"Friend Visākha, the noble eightfold path is conditioned."

"Ayye, are the three heaps included within the noble eightfold path, or is the
noble eightfold path included within the three heaps?"

"Friend Visākha, the three heaps are not included within the noble eightfold
path. Rather, the noble eightfold path is included within the three heaps.

Right speech, right action, and right livelihood are included within the
conduct heap.

Right effort, right remembering, and right mental composure are included
within the mental-composure heap.

Right view and right attitude are included within the discernment heap."

### Mental Composure

"Ayye, what is mental composure? What dhammas are signs of mental composure?
What dhammas are the equipment of mental composure? What is the development of
mental composure?"

"Friend Visākha, directness of the feeling mind is mental composure.

The four establishments of sati are signs of mental composure.

The four right exertions are the equipment of mental composure.

The repetition, development, and making-much of these same dhammas is the
development of mental composure here."

### Three Conditioners

"Ayye, how many conditioners are there?"

"Friend Visākha, there are these three conditioners:

- the body conditioner
- the verbal process
- the mind conditioner"

"Ayye, what is the body conditioner? What is the verbal process? What is the
mind conditioner?"

"Friend Visākha, in-breathing and out-breathing are the body conditioner.

Thinking and pondering are the verbal process.

Recognition and felt experience are the mind conditioner."

"Ayye, why are in-breathing and out-breathing the body conditioner? Why are
thinking and pondering the verbal process? Why are recognition and felt
experience the mind conditioner?"

"Friend Visākha, in-breathing and out-breathing are bodily dhammas, bound up
with the body. That is why in-breathing and out-breathing are the body
conditioner.

First one thinks and ponders, and afterward one breaks into speech. That is
why thinking and pondering are the verbal process.

Recognition and felt experience are mental dhammas, bound up with the feeling
mind. That is why recognition and felt experience are the mind conditioner."

### Attainment of Cessation

"Ayye, how does the attainment of cessation of recognition and felt experience
come to be?"

"Friend Visākha, for a bhikkhu entering the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience, it does not occur:

'I will enter the attainment of cessation of recognition and felt experience,'
or 'I am entering the attainment of cessation of recognition and felt
experience,' or 'I have entered the attainment of cessation of recognition and
felt experience.'

Rather, that bhikkhu's feeling mind has already been developed in such a way
that it leads to that state."

"Ayye, for a bhikkhu entering the attainment of cessation of recognition and
felt experience, which dhammas are quenched first: the body conditioner, the
verbal process, or the mind conditioner?"

"Friend Visākha, for a bhikkhu entering the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience, the verbal process is quenched first, then
the body conditioner, then the mind conditioner."

"Ayye, how does emergence from the attainment of cessation of recognition and
felt experience come to be?"

"Friend Visākha, for a bhikkhu emerging from the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience, it does not occur:

'I will emerge from the attainment of cessation of recognition and felt
experience,' or 'I am emerging from the attainment of cessation of recognition
and felt experience,' or 'I have emerged from the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience.'

Rather, that bhikkhu's feeling mind has already been developed in such a way
that it leads to that state."

"Ayye, for a bhikkhu emerging from the attainment of cessation of recognition
and felt experience, which dhammas arise first: the body conditioner, the
verbal process, or the mind conditioner?"

"Friend Visākha, for a bhikkhu emerging from the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience, the mind conditioner arises first, then the
body conditioner, then the verbal process."

"Ayye, how many contacts touch a bhikkhu who has emerged from the attainment
of cessation of recognition and felt experience?"

"Friend Visākha, three contacts touch a bhikkhu who has emerged from the
attainment of cessation of recognition and felt experience:

- emptiness contact
- signless contact
- wishless contact"

"Ayye, for a bhikkhu who has emerged from the attainment of cessation of
recognition and felt experience, toward what is the feeling mind inclined,
sloping, and leaning?"

"Friend Visākha, for a bhikkhu who has emerged from the attainment of
cessation of recognition and felt experience, the feeling mind is inclined to
seclusion, slopes to seclusion, and leans to seclusion."

### Three Kinds of Felt Experience

"Ayye, how many kinds of felt experience are there?"

"Friend Visākha, there are these three kinds of felt experience:

- pleasant feeling
- painful feeling
- mixed feeling"

"Ayye, what is pleasant feeling? What is painful feeling? What is mixed
feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, whatever bodily or mental satisfaction, whatever agreeable
felt experience is felt: this is pleasant feeling.

Whatever bodily or mental dissatisfaction, whatever disagreeable felt
experience is felt: this is painful feeling.

Whatever bodily or mental felt experience is neither agreeable nor
disagreeable: this is mixed feeling."

"Ayye, in what way is pleasant feeling satisfying, and in what way is it
dissatisfying? In what way is painful feeling satisfying, and in what way is
it dissatisfying? In what way is mixed feeling satisfying, and in what way is
it dissatisfying?"

"Friend Visākha, pleasant feeling is satisfying while it stays and
dissatisfying when it changes.

Painful feeling is dissatisfying while it stays and satisfying when it
changes.

Mixed feeling is satisfying when there is knowledge and dissatisfying when
there is no knowledge."

### Felt Experience and Underlying Tendencies

"Ayye, what underlying tendency lies underneath pleasant feeling? What
underlying tendency lies underneath painful feeling? What underlying tendency
lies underneath mixed feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, the underlying tendency toward passion lies underneath
pleasant feeling.

The underlying tendency toward resistance lies underneath painful feeling.

The underlying tendency toward ignorance lies underneath mixed feeling."

"Ayye, does the underlying tendency toward passion lie underneath every
pleasant feeling? Does the underlying tendency toward resistance lie underneath
every painful feeling? Does the underlying tendency toward ignorance lie
underneath every mixed feeling?"

"No, friend Visākha. The underlying tendency toward passion does not lie
underneath every pleasant feeling. The underlying tendency toward resistance
does not lie underneath every painful feeling. The underlying tendency toward
ignorance does not lie underneath every mixed feeling."

"Ayye, what should be abandoned in relation to pleasant feeling? What should be
abandoned in relation to painful feeling? What should be abandoned in relation
to mixed feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, the underlying tendency toward passion should be abandoned in
relation to pleasant feeling.

The underlying tendency toward resistance should be abandoned in relation to
painful feeling.

The underlying tendency toward ignorance should be abandoned in relation to
mixed feeling."

"Ayye, should the underlying tendency toward passion be abandoned in relation
to every pleasant feeling? Should the underlying tendency toward resistance be
abandoned in relation to every painful feeling? Should the underlying tendency
toward ignorance be abandoned in relation to every mixed feeling?"

"No, friend Visākha. The underlying tendency toward passion should not be
abandoned in relation to every pleasant feeling. The underlying tendency toward
resistance should not be abandoned in relation to every painful feeling. The
underlying tendency toward ignorance should not be abandoned in relation to
every mixed feeling.

Here, friend Visākha, quite secluded from sensuality, secluded from unwholesome
dhammas, a bhikkhu enters and remains in the first mental theme, with thinking
and pondering, with delight and ease born of seclusion.

Through that, one abandons passion, and the underlying tendency toward passion
does not lie there.

Here, friend Visākha, a bhikkhu reflects:

'When will I enter and remain in that field which the noble ones now enter and
remain in?'

As one sets up longing for unsurpassed releases, distress arises with longing
as condition.

Through that, one abandons resistance, and the underlying tendency toward
resistance does not lie there.

Here, friend Visākha, with the abandoning of ease and discomfort, and with the
earlier ending of gladness and distress, a bhikkhu enters and remains in the
fourth mental theme, without discomfort and without ease, with purity of
remembering through dynamic balance.

Through that, one abandons ignorance, and the underlying tendency toward
ignorance does not lie there."

### Counterparts

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of pleasant feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, painful feeling is the counterpart of pleasant feeling."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of painful feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, pleasant feeling is the counterpart of painful feeling."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of mixed feeling?"

"Friend Visākha, ignorance is the counterpart of mixed feeling."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of ignorance?"

"Friend Visākha, clear knowledge is the counterpart of ignorance."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of clear knowledge?"

"Friend Visākha, release is the counterpart of clear knowledge."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of release?"

"Friend Visākha, nibbāna is the counterpart of release."

"Ayye, what is the counterpart of nibbāna?"

"You have gone too far with the question, friend Visākha. You could not grasp
the limit of questions.

Friend Visākha, the spiritual life is grounded in nibbāna, directed to
nibbāna, and completed in nibbāna.

If you wish, friend Visākha, you should go to the Buddha and ask him about
this matter. You should remember it just as the Buddha answers."

### The Buddha Confirms Dhammadinnā

Then Visākha the lay follower was glad and approved of Dhammadinnā the
bhikkhunī's words. He rose from his seat, bowed to her, and respectfully
circled her, keeping her to his right. Then he went to the Buddha, bowed, and
sat to one side.

Sitting there, he reported to the Buddha the whole conversation he had had
with Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī.

When this was said, the Buddha said to him:

"Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī is wise, Visākha. Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī has
great discernment.

If you had asked me about this matter, I would have answered it in exactly the
same way that Dhammadinnā the bhikkhunī answered it.

That is its meaning. Remember it like this."

That is what the Buddha said.

Visākha the lay follower was satisfied and welcomed what the Buddha had said.

The Cūḷavedalla Sutta, the fourth discourse, is finished.
