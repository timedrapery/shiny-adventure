## Translation

### The Moonlit Night

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Rājagaha, in Jīvaka Komārabhacca's
Mango Grove, together with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 bhikkhus.

At that time it was the Komudī full moon, the fifteenth day of the fourth
month, and King Ajātasattu of Magadha, son of the Videhan princess, was
sitting upstairs in the royal palace surrounded by his ministers.

Then King Ajātasattu spoke from the heart:

"This moonlit night is so delightful, so beautiful, so glorious, so lovely.
What ascetic or brahmin should I visit tonight, so that my mind might find
peace?"

Each of the king's ministers suggested a famous teacher:

- Pūraṇa Kassapa
- Makkhali Gosāla
- Ajita Kesakambalī
- Pakudha Kaccāyana
- Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta
- Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta

After each suggestion, the king remained silent.

### Jīvaka's Suggestion

At that time Jīvaka Komārabhacca was sitting silently nearby.

Then the king said to him, "My dear Jīvaka, why are you silent?"

"Great king, the Blessed One, the arahant, the fully awakened Buddha, is
staying in my mango grove together with a large Saṅgha of 1,250 bhikkhus.
He has this good reputation:

'That Blessed One is the arahant, the fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in
knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, unsurpassed guide for those
fit for training, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.'

Let Your Majesty visit him. Perhaps in doing so your mind will find peace."

"Well then, my dear Jīvaka, have the elephants prepared."

"Yes, great king," Jīvaka replied.

He had five hundred female elephants made ready, along with the king's riding
elephant, and informed the king when all was prepared.

Then King Ajātasattu had women mounted on each of the five hundred female
elephants, while he mounted his own elephant. With attendants carrying
torches, he set out from Rājagaha in full royal display toward Jīvaka's Mango
Grove.

But when he came near the grove, the king grew frightened, alarmed, and
shaken.

He said to Jīvaka:

"My dear Jīvaka, I hope you are not deceiving me. I hope you are not betraying
me. I hope you are not handing me over to my enemies. For how could there be
no sound of coughing or throat-clearing, no noise at all, in such a large
Saṅgha of 1,250 bhikkhus?"

"Do not be afraid, great king, do not be afraid. I am not deceiving you,
betraying you, or handing you over to your enemies. Go forward, great king,
go forward. Those are the lamps shining in the pavilion."

### Ajātasattu Meets the Buddha

The king rode his elephant as far as the ground allowed, then got down and
approached the pavilion on foot.

At the door he asked Jīvaka, "My dear Jīvaka, where is the Buddha?"

"That is the Buddha, great king, that is the Buddha. He is sitting against
the central pillar, facing east, in front of the Saṅgha."

Then the king approached the Buddha and stood to one side. Looking over the
Saṅgha of bhikkhus, so quiet, like a still and clear lake, he spoke from the
heart:

"May my son, Prince Udāyibhadda, have such peace as this Saṅgha of bhikkhus
enjoys now."

"Has your mind gone to one you love, great king?"

"Bhante, I love my son, Prince Udāyibhadda. May he have such peace as this
Saṅgha of bhikkhus enjoys now."

Then the king bowed to the Buddha, raised joined palms toward the Saṅgha, and
sat down to one side.

### The Question

The king said:

"Bhante, I would like to ask the Blessed One about a certain matter, if the
Blessed One would allow it."

"Ask what you wish, great king."

"Bhante, there are many kinds of work in the world, such as elephant-trainers,
horsemen, charioteers, archers, standard-bearers, attendants, cooks, princes,
warriors, bakers, barbers, bath attendants, garland-makers, dyers,
embroiderers, basket-makers, potters, accountants, tally-keepers, and many
other crafts besides. The people who follow those occupations enjoy a fruit
of their work visible in this very life. By that they make themselves happy,
and they make their parents, children, partners, friends, and companions
happy. They also establish a gift to ascetics and brahmins, a wholesome
offering that leads to heaven and ripens in happiness.

Can the Blessed One point out a fruit of the ascetic life that is likewise
visible in this very life?"

"Great king, do you remember asking this question of other ascetics and
brahmins?"

"I do, bhante."

"Then, great king, if you do not mind, tell me how they answered."

"It is no trouble when the Blessed One is sitting here."

"Then speak, great king."

### The Teachers Ajātasattu Consulted

#### Pūraṇa Kassapa

"Bhante, once I approached Pūraṇa Kassapa, exchanged greetings with him, sat
down to one side, and asked him this same question.

He said:

'Great king, one who acts does no evil in killing, cutting, mutilating,
torturing, oppressing, frightening, stealing, breaking in, plundering,
robbing the road, committing adultery, or lying. Even if someone were to turn
all living beings on this earth into one heap of flesh with a razor-sharp
wheel, no evil would come of it and no evil result. Even if someone were to
go along the south bank of the Ganges killing and making others kill, cutting
and making others cut, torturing and making others torture, no evil would
come of it and no evil result. Even if someone were to go along the north
bank of the Ganges giving and sacrificing and making others give and
sacrifice, no merit would come of it and no result of merit. In giving,
self-restraint, restraint, and truthfulness there is no merit and no result
of merit.'

So when I asked Pūraṇa Kassapa about a fruit of the ascetic life visible in
this very life, he answered with the doctrine of non-action. It was like
someone answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I thought:
'How could someone like me rebuke an ascetic or brahmin living in my realm?'
So I neither approved nor rejected what Pūraṇa Kassapa said. Displeased, but
without showing displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I
got up from my seat and left."

#### Makkhali Gosāla

"Then I approached Makkhali Gosāla and asked him the same question.

He said:

'Great king, there is no cause and no condition for the corruption of beings.
Beings are corrupted without cause and without condition. There is no cause
and no condition for the purification of beings. Beings are purified without
cause and without condition. There is no self-will, no other-will, no human
strength or energy. All beings, all living creatures, all persons, all souls
are helpless, powerless, and fixed by destiny, circumstance, and nature.
They experience pleasure and pain through the six classes of rebirth.

The fool and the wise alike wander on through fixed rounds of births, deeds,
realms, and great eons before they make an end of dissatisfaction. There is
no such thing as:
'By this conduct, observance, austerity, or spiritual practice I shall ripen
unripened kamma or wear away old kamma little by little.'
That cannot be. Pleasure and pain are allotted. There is no increase and no
decrease, no better and no worse. It is like a ball of string that rolls away
as it unravels. In the same way, the fool and the wise wander on and finally
make an end of dissatisfaction.'

So when I asked Makkhali Gosāla about a fruit of the ascetic life visible in
this very life, he answered with purification through wandering on. It was
like someone answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I neither approved nor rejected what he said. Displeased, but without showing
displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I got up from my
seat and left."

#### Ajita Kesakambalī

"Then I approached Ajita Kesakambalī and asked him the same question.

He said:

'Great king, there is no meaning in giving, sacrifice, or offerings. There is
no fruit or result of good and bad actions. There is no afterlife. There is
no mother or father in any meaningful sense. There are no beings reborn
spontaneously. There is no ascetic or brahmin rightly practicing who can
describe the afterlife after realizing it directly for oneself.

This person is made up of the four principal states. At death, the earth in
the body returns to earth, the water to water, the fire to fire, and the air
to air. The faculties vanish into space. Four men carry the corpse away on a
bier. Their footprints show the way to the cremation ground. The bones are
bleached, and offerings end in ashes. Giving is the doctrine of fools. Those
who affirm a positive doctrine speak only empty, false nonsense. When the
body breaks up, both the fool and the wise are annihilated and destroyed.
After death they do not exist.'

So when I asked Ajita Kesakambalī about a fruit of the ascetic life visible
in this very life, he answered with annihilationism. It was like someone
answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I neither approved nor rejected what he said. Displeased, but without showing
displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I got up from my
seat and left."

#### Pakudha Kaccāyana

"Then I approached Pakudha Kaccāyana and asked him the same question.

He said:

'Great king, there are these seven substances that are unmade, unfashioned,
not created, without a creator, barren, steady like a mountain peak, fixed
like a pillar. They do not move, change, or obstruct one another. They cannot
make pleasure, pain, or both pleasure and pain for one another. What seven?
The substances of earth, water, fire, air, pleasure, pain, and soul as the
seventh.

There is no one who kills or causes killing, no one who hears or causes
hearing, no one who understands or causes understanding. If someone cuts off
another's head with a sharp sword, no one takes a life. The sword only passes
through the gap between the seven substances.'

So when I asked Pakudha Kaccāyana about a fruit of the ascetic life visible
in this very life, he answered with something else entirely. It was like
someone answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I neither approved nor rejected what he said. Displeased, but without showing
displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I got up from my
seat and left."

#### Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta

"Then I approached Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta and asked him the same question.

He said:

'Great king, consider a Nigaṇṭha restrained by a fourfold restraint. And how
is a Nigaṇṭha restrained by a fourfold restraint? One is restrained in all
that should be restrained, bridled in all that should be bridled, washed of
evil in all that should be washed, and checked in all that should be checked.
When restrained by this fourfold restraint, one is called knotless,
self-mastered, self-controlled, and steadfast.'

So when I asked Nigaṇṭha Nātaputta about a fruit of the ascetic life visible
in this very life, he answered with the fourfold restraint. It was like
someone answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I neither approved nor rejected what he said. Displeased, but without showing
displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I got up from my
seat and left."

#### Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta

"Then I approached Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta and asked him the same question.

He said:

'If you ask me whether there is another world, if I thought it were so I
would say so. But I do not say it is so. I do not say it is otherwise. I do
not say it is not so. I do not say it is not not so.

The same applies if you ask whether there is no other world, whether there
both is and is not another world, or whether there neither is nor is not
another world; whether there are beings spontaneously reborn, whether there
are not, both are and are not, or neither are nor are not; whether there is a
fruit and result of good and bad deeds, whether there is not, whether there
both is and is not, or whether there neither is nor is not; and whether a
Tathāgata exists after death, does not exist, both exists and does not exist,
or neither exists nor does not exist after death. If I thought any of these
were so, I would say so. But I do not say it is so, otherwise, not so, or not
not so.'

So when I asked Sañjaya Belaṭṭhiputta about a fruit of the ascetic life
visible in this very life, he answered with evasive uncertainty. It was like
someone answering with breadfruit when asked about a mango.

I thought:
'This is the most foolish and stupid of all these ascetics and brahmins. How
could he answer with evasive uncertainty when asked about a fruit of the
ascetic life visible in this very life?'

Still, I neither approved nor rejected what he said. Displeased, but without
showing displeasure, neither accepting nor contradicting his words, I got up
from my seat and left.

So now, bhante, I ask the Blessed One the same question."

### The First Fruit of the Ascetic Life

"Great king, I can point out a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life.

But first I will ask you a question, and answer as you think fit.

What do you think, great king? Suppose you had a servant, a worker, who rose
before you, lay down after you, was obedient, pleasant in conduct, courteous
in speech, and watched your face for orders.

Suppose that servant were to think:
'The result of good actions is truly astonishing. For King Ajātasattu is a
human being, and so am I. Yet he enjoys himself supplied with the five kinds
of sensual stimulation like a god, while I am his servant, his worker, rising
before him and lying down after him, obedient, pleasant, courteous, and
watching his face for orders. Why don't I do good and shave off hair and
beard, put on ocher robes, and go forth from the lay life to homelessness?'

After some time, suppose that is exactly what he does. Having gone forth, he
lives restrained in body, speech, and mind, content with nothing more than
robes and almsfood, delighting in seclusion.

If your men reported this to you, would you say:
'Bring that man back to me. Let him once more be my servant and worker'?"

"No, bhante. Rather, I would bow to him, rise for him, offer him a seat, and
invite him to accept robes, almsfood, lodgings, and medicines and supplies
for the sick. I would arrange for his lawful guarding and protection."

"What do you think, great king? If this is so, is there a fruit of the
ascetic life visible in this very life or not?"

"Clearly there is, bhante."

"This is the first fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very life that I
point out to you."

### The Second Fruit of the Ascetic Life

"Can the Blessed One point out another fruit of the ascetic life visible in
this very life?"

"I can, great king.

Again, suppose there were a farmer or householder, a hard worker, someone who
built up wealth. Suppose that person were to think:
'The result of good actions is truly astonishing. For King Ajātasattu is a
human being, and so am I. Yet he enjoys himself supplied with the five kinds
of sensual stimulation like a god, while I am a farmer or householder, a hard
worker building up wealth. Why don't I shave off hair and beard, put on ocher
robes, and go forth from the lay life to homelessness?'

After some time, suppose that person gives up a small or great fortune, gives
up a small or great circle of relatives, shaves off hair and beard, puts on
ocher robes, and goes forth from the lay life to homelessness. Having gone
forth, one lives restrained in body, speech, and mind, content with nothing
more than robes and almsfood, delighting in seclusion.

If your men reported this to you, would you say:
'Bring that person back to me. Let that one once more be a householder,
working hard and building up wealth'?"

"No, bhante. Rather, I would bow to such a person, rise for that person,
offer a seat, and invite that person to accept robes, almsfood, lodgings, and
medicines and supplies for the sick. I would arrange lawful guarding and
protection."

"What do you think, great king? If this is so, is there a fruit of the
ascetic life visible in this very life or not?"

"Clearly there is, bhante."

"This is the second fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very life that
I point out to you."

### The Higher Fruits

"But, bhante, is there a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very life
that is better and finer than these?"

"I can point that out, great king. Listen carefully, attend well, and I will
speak."

"Yes, bhante," the king replied.

The Buddha said this:

"Great king, a Tathāgata arises in the world, the arahant, the fully awakened
Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world,
unsurpassed guide for those fit for training, teacher of gods and humans,
awakened, blessed.

Having realized with direct knowledge this world, with its gods, Māras, and
Brahmās, this generation with its ascetics and brahmins, rulers and people,
he makes it known to others. He teaches a Dhamma that is good in the
beginning, good in the middle, and good in the end, meaningful and
well-phrased. He reveals a spiritual life that is completely full and pure.

A householder, a householder's child, or someone born in a good family hears
that Dhamma. Hearing it, one gains confidence in the Tathāgata and reflects:

'Life at home is crowded and dusty. Going forth is wide open. It is not easy
for one living at home to live the spiritual life utterly full and pure, as
if polished like a shell. Why don't I shave off hair and beard, put on ocher
robes, and go forth from the lay life to homelessness?'

After some time, one gives up a small or great fortune, gives up a small or
great circle of relatives, shaves off hair and beard, puts on ocher robes,
and goes forth from the lay life to homelessness.

Once gone forth, one lives restrained by restraint in the pātimokkha, of good
conduct and proper resort, seeing danger in the slightest fault, training by
undertaking the training rules. One acts skillfully by body and speech, is
purified in livelihood, accomplished in conduct, guards the faculties, has
remembering and clear knowing, and is content.

### Conduct

And how, great king, is a bhikkhu accomplished in conduct?

One gives up:

- killing living beings, setting aside rod and sword, living scrupulous and
  kind, full of sympathy for all living beings
- taking what is not given, taking only what is given, expecting only what is
  given, living cleanly, not thieving
- unchaste conduct, living the celibate life, set apart, avoiding sexual
  activity
- false speech, speaking truth, holding to truth, reliable and dependable,
  not deceiving the world
- divisive speech, not repeating in one place what was heard elsewhere to
  create division, but reconciling the divided and supporting the united,
  delighting in harmony, loving harmony, speaking words that make harmony
- harsh speech, speaking words that are gentle, pleasing to the ear,
  affectionate, going to the heart, courteous, agreeable, and liked by many
- idle chatter, speaking at the proper time what is true, beneficial, and in
  line with the Dhamma and training, words worth treasuring, timely,
  measured, and useful

One refrains from:

- injuring seeds and plants
- eating at the wrong time, taking one meal a day and not eating at night
- seeing shows of dancing, singing, and music
- adornment with garlands, scents, and makeup
- high and luxurious beds
- accepting gold and money
- accepting raw grain, raw meat, women and girls, male and female servants,
  goats and sheep, chickens and pigs, elephants, cattle, horses and mares,
  or fields and land
- running errands and carrying messages
- buying and selling
- falsifying weights, metals, and measures
- bribery, fraud, cheating, and trickery
- mutilation, killing, capture, banditry, plunder, and violence

This is the shorter section on conduct.

One also refrains from further kinds of behavior that others still engage in
while living on food given in faith:

- injuring plants and seeds of all kinds
- storing up food, drink, clothes, vehicles, bedding, scents, and fleshly
  goods for personal use
- watching entertainments such as dances, songs, music, performances,
  storytellers, acrobatics, corpse-worker displays, and contests of animals,
  fighters, and troops
- games and gambling that breed negligence
- high and luxurious bedding of many elaborate kinds
- beautifying and adorning the body with ointments, mirrors, ornaments,
  perfumes, jewelry, parasols, fine sandals, and decorated cloth
- low talk about kings, ministers, armies, food, drink, clothes, beds,
  families, villages, towns, cities, lands, women, heroes, the dead, gossip,
  tales of land and sea, and rebirth here or there
- arguments and debating talk of the sort that tries to trap and defeat
  others
- errands and messages for rulers, ministers, nobles, brahmins,
  householders, and princes
- fawning, flattering, hinting, belittling, and using material things to
  pursue more material things

This is the middle section on conduct.

One also refrains from wrong livelihood through low arts and low lore, such
as:

- augury, omen-reading, interpreting portents and dreams, reading bodily
  marks, mouse-chewed cloth, and sacrificial signs
- spells, exorcisms, snake-magic, poison lore, and predictions based on wild
  animals
- reading marks on gems, cloth, weapons, people, servants, and animals
- predictions of war, victory, defeat, eclipses, celestial events,
  earthquakes, storms, rain, drought, abundance, famine, safety, danger,
  sickness, and health
- calculating, accounting, poetry, and cosmology used as livelihood
- marriage arrangements, divorces, wedding rites, charms, tongue-binding,
  jaw-locking, hand-charms, ear-charms, mirror-oracles, girl-oracles,
  spirit-oracles, worship of the sun, rituals for luck, and fire rites
- propitiation rites, wish-granting rites, rites for ghosts, the earth, rain,
  settlements, and house consecrations
- medical and surgical practice as livelihood, including purgatives,
  emetics, expectorants, oils, salves, eye-medicines, nasal treatment, needle
  and scalpel work, children's remedies, roots, and herbal bandages

Seeing no danger anywhere with regard to this ethical restraint, a bhikkhu
experiences blameless ease within. That is how a bhikkhu is accomplished in
conduct.

### Guarding the Faculties

And how does a bhikkhu guard the faculties?

When seeing a visible form with the eye, one does not get caught up in its
features and details. If the eye-faculty were left unguarded, harmful and
unwholesome states of coveting and distress would flood in. So one practices
restraint, guards the eye-faculty, and reaches restraint with regard to it.

The same applies when hearing a sound with the ear, smelling an odor with the
nose, tasting a flavor with the tongue, experiencing a touch with the body,
and knowing a dhamma with the mind.

When one has this noble guarding of the faculties, one experiences unsullied
ease within. That is how a bhikkhu guards the faculties.

### Remembering and Clear Knowing

And how does a bhikkhu have remembering and clear knowing?

One acts with clear knowing when going forward and coming back, when looking
ahead and looking away, when bending and stretching, when carrying outer robe,
bowl, and robes, when eating, drinking, chewing, and tasting, when urinating
and defecating, when walking, standing, sitting, falling asleep, waking,
speaking, and keeping silent.

That is how a bhikkhu has remembering and clear knowing.

### Contentment

And how is a bhikkhu content?

One is content with robes to protect the body and with almsfood to support the
belly. Wherever one goes, one sets out taking only these along.

It is like a bird: wherever it flies, it goes carrying only its wings.

In the same way, a bhikkhu is content with robes to protect the body and with
almsfood to support the belly. Wherever one goes, one sets out taking only
these along.

That is how a bhikkhu is content.

### Clearing the Five Distractions

When a bhikkhu possesses this full range of noble conduct, this noble
guarding of the faculties, this noble remembering and clear knowing, and this
noble contentment, one resorts to a secluded lodging: the wilderness, the
root of a tree, a mountain, a ravine, a hillside cave, a charnel ground, a
forest thicket, the open air, or a heap of straw.

After returning from almsround, after the meal, one sits down cross-legged,
keeps the body upright, and establishes remembering before oneself.

Giving up coveting for the world, one abides with a mind free of coveting,
cleansing the mind of coveting.

Giving up ill will and malice, one abides with a mind free of ill will, full
of sympathy for all living beings, cleansing the mind of ill will.

Giving up dullness and drowsiness, one abides free from dullness and
drowsiness, perceiving light, remembering and clearly knowing, cleansing the
mind of dullness and drowsiness.

Giving up agitation and regret, one abides unagitated, inwardly at peace,
cleansing the mind of agitation and regret.

Giving up doubt, one abides having crossed beyond doubt, no longer uncertain
about wholesome qualities, cleansing the mind of doubt.

As long as these five distractions have not been abandoned within, a bhikkhu
regards them as:

- a debt
- a disease
- a prison
- slavery
- a desert crossing

But when these five distractions have been abandoned within, a bhikkhu
regards that as:

- freedom from debt
- good health
- release from prison
- freedom
- a place of safety

Seeing that the distractions have been abandoned, gladness arises. For one who
is glad, delight arises. When the mind is delighted, the body becomes calm.
When the body is calm, one feels ease. Feeling ease, the mind becomes mentally
composed.

### The First Mental Theme

Quite secluded from sensuality, secluded from unwholesome dhammas, a bhikkhu
enters and remains in the first mental theme, with thinking and pondering,
with delight and ease born of seclusion.

One drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very body with delight and
ease born of seclusion, so that no part of the whole body remains untouched by
delight and ease born of seclusion.

It is like a skilled bath attendant or apprentice who would pour bath powder
into a bronze bowl and knead it, sprinkling it little by little with water,
until the ball of bath powder was soaked and saturated inside and out, yet not
dripping.

In the same way, a bhikkhu drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very
body with delight and ease born of seclusion.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Second Mental Theme

With the subsiding of thinking and pondering, with inner clarity and
unification of mind, a bhikkhu enters and remains in the second mental theme,
without thinking and pondering, with delight and ease born of mental
composure.

One drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very body with delight and ease
born of mental composure, so that no part of the whole body remains untouched
by delight and ease born of mental composure.

It is like a deep lake fed by spring water, with no inlet from east, west,
north, or south, and with the heavens not sending rain from time to time; yet
the stream of cool water welling up inside the lake drenches, steeps, fills,
and suffuses it, so that no part of the whole lake remains untouched by cool
water.

In the same way, a bhikkhu drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very
body with delight and ease born of mental composure.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Third Mental Theme

With the fading of delight, a bhikkhu remains in dynamic balance, remembering
and clearly knowing, and experiences ease in the body. One enters and remains
in the third mental theme, of which the noble ones say:
'One of dynamic balance and remembering stays at ease.'

One drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very body with ease free from
delight, so that no part of the whole body remains untouched by ease free from
delight.

It is like a pool of blue, red, or white lotuses. Some lotuses are born and
grow in the water without rising above it, and from tip to root they are
drenched, steeped, filled, and suffused by cool water, so that no part of
them remains untouched by cool water.

In the same way, a bhikkhu drenches, steeps, fills, and suffuses this very
body with ease free from delight.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Fourth Mental Theme

With the abandoning of ease and discomfort, and with the earlier ending of
gladness and sadness, a bhikkhu enters and remains in the fourth mental theme,
without discomfort and without ease, purified by dynamic balance and
remembering.

One sits suffusing this very body with a pure and bright mind, so that no part
of the whole body remains untouched by a pure and bright mind.

It is like a person sitting wrapped from head to foot in a white cloth, so
that no part of the whole body remains uncovered by the white cloth.

In the same way, a bhikkhu sits suffusing this very body with a pure and
bright mind.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### Knowledge and Seeing

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward knowledge and seeing.

One understands:
'This body of mine is form, made of the four principal states, produced by
mother and father, built up from rice and porridge, liable to impermanence,
wearing away, erosion, breaking, and destruction. And this knowing of mine is
supported here and tied to it.'

It is like a beryl gem, beautiful, eight-faceted, well-worked, transparent,
clear, and flawless, strung with a blue, yellow, red, white, or brown thread.
A person with clear sight, taking it in hand and examining it, would know:
'This is a beautiful beryl gem, eight-faceted, well-worked, transparent,
clear, and flawless, strung with a blue, yellow, red, white, or brown
thread.'

In the same way, a bhikkhu understands this body and this knowing as they
really are.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### A Mind-Made Body

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward creating a mind-made body.

From this body one creates another body, formed, mind-made, complete in all
its larger and smaller limbs, lacking no faculty.

It is like drawing:

- a reed from its sheath
- a sword from its scabbard
- a snake from its slough

In each case one can see that the two are different, and that one has been
drawn out from the other.

In the same way, from this body one creates another body, formed, mind-made,
complete in all its larger and smaller limbs, lacking no faculty.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### Psychic Powers

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward psychic power.

One wields many kinds of psychic power:

- becoming one from many, and many from one
- appearing and disappearing
- going unhindered through walls, ramparts, and mountains as if through space
- diving in and out of the earth as if it were water
- walking on water as if it were land
- flying cross-legged through the sky like a bird
- touching and stroking the sun and moon with the hand, so mighty and
  powerful
- exercising mastery with the body as far as the Brahmā worlds

It is like a skilled potter with well-prepared clay, a skilled ivory-worker
with well-prepared ivory, or a skilled goldsmith with well-prepared gold:
each could make from the material whatever kind of item one wished.

In the same way, a bhikkhu wields many kinds of psychic power.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Divine Ear

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward the divine ear.

With the divine ear, purified and beyond the human, one hears both kinds of
sounds, human and divine, whether far or near.

It is like a traveler on the road who hears the sound of drums, clay drums,
horns, kettledrums, and tom-toms, and knows:
'That is the sound of drums, that is the sound of clay drums, that is the
sound of horns, kettledrums, and tom-toms.'

In the same way, a bhikkhu hears both kinds of sounds, human and divine,
whether far or near.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### Knowing Others' Minds

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward encompassing the minds of others.

Having encompassed the minds of other beings and individuals with one's own
mind, one understands:

- mind with passion as `mind with passion`, and mind without passion as `mind
  without passion`
- mind with aversion as `mind with aversion`, and mind without aversion as
  `mind without aversion`
- mind with delusion as `mind with delusion`, and mind without delusion as
  `mind without delusion`
- contracted mind as `contracted mind`, and distracted mind as `distracted
  mind`
- expansive mind as `expansive mind`, and unexpansive mind as `unexpansive
  mind`
- surpassable mind as `surpassable mind`, and unsurpassable mind as
  `unsurpassable mind`
- mentally composed mind as `mentally composed mind`, and uncomposed mind as
  `uncomposed mind`
- freed mind as `freed mind`, and unfreed mind as `unfreed mind`

It is like a young woman or man, youthful and fond of adornment, examining
one's own face in a bright mirror or a clear bowl of water. If there were a
blemish, one would know: 'There is a blemish in me.' If there were no blemish,
one would know: 'There is no blemish in me.'

In the same way, a bhikkhu understands the minds of other beings and
individuals after encompassing them with one's own mind.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### Recollecting Past Lives

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward recollecting past lives.

One recollects many kinds of past lives: one birth, two births, three births,
four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, a hundred, a thousand, a
hundred thousand; many eons of world-contraction, many eons of
world-expansion, many eons of world-contraction and expansion.

One remembers:
'There I was named this, of such a clan, with such an appearance; that was my
food, that was how I experienced pleasure and pain, that was the span of my
life. Passing away from there, I was reborn elsewhere. There too I was named
this, of such a clan, with such an appearance; that was my food, that was how
I experienced pleasure and pain, that was the span of my life. Passing away
from there, I was reborn here.'

Thus one recollects many kinds of past lives, with aspects and particulars.

It is like a person who goes from one's own village to another village, from
that village to still another village, and from there returns home, later
thinking:
'I went from my village to that village. There I stood like this, sat like
that, spoke like this, kept silent like that. From there I went to another
village. There too I stood like this, sat like that, spoke like this, kept
silent like that. From there I returned home.'

In the same way, a bhikkhu recollects many kinds of past lives, with aspects
and particulars.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Divine Eye

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward the knowledge of the passing away and reappearance of beings.

With the purified divine eye, beyond the human, one sees beings passing away
and reappearing, low and high, beautiful and ugly, in good destinations and
bad destinations. One understands beings as moving on according to their
kamma:

'These beings did bad things by body, speech, and mind. They criticized the
noble ones, held wrong view, and acted from wrong view. When the body broke
up, after death, they were reborn in a plane of loss, a bad destination, the
underworld, hell.

But these beings did good things by body, speech, and mind. They did not
criticize the noble ones, held right view, and acted from right view. When
the body broke up, after death, they were reborn in a good destination, a
heavenly world.'

Thus, with the purified divine eye, beyond the human, one sees beings passing
away and reappearing, low and high, beautiful and ugly, in good destinations
and bad destinations, understanding them as moving on according to their
kamma.

It is like a person with clear eyesight standing in an upper hall at a
crossroads, seeing people entering a house, leaving it, walking along the
roads, and sitting in the square, and knowing exactly what they are doing.

In the same way, a bhikkhu sees beings passing away and reappearing according
to their kamma.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones.

### The Wearing Away of the Outflows

When the mind is mentally composed like this, purified, bright, stainless,
free from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and unshakable, a bhikkhu
turns it toward the knowledge of the wearing away of the outflows.

One directly understands as it really is:

- `This is dissatisfaction.`
- `This is the origin of dissatisfaction.`
- `This is the quenching of dissatisfaction.`
- `This is the path of practice leading to the quenching of dissatisfaction.`

One directly understands as it really is:

- `These are the outflows.`
- `This is the origin of the outflows.`
- `This is the quenching of the outflows.`
- `This is the path of practice leading to the quenching of the outflows.`

Knowing and seeing like this, the mind is freed from the outflow of
sensuality, the outflow of becoming, and the outflow of ignorance.

When it is freed, there comes the knowledge:
'It is freed.'

One discerns:
'Birth is worn away, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done
has been done, there is no more of this state of being.'

It is like a mountain lake, transparent, clear, and stainless. A person with
clear eyesight standing on the shore would see shells, gravel, and schools of
fish swimming or staying still, and would know exactly what is there.

In the same way, a bhikkhu directly understands the wearing away of the
outflows.

This too, great king, is a fruit of the ascetic life visible in this very
life, better and finer than the former ones. And there is no other fruit of
the ascetic life visible in this very life better or finer than this."

### Ajātasattu Takes Refuge

When the Buddha had spoken, King Ajātasattu said:

"Excellent, bhante! Excellent, bhante!

Just as if someone were to set upright what had been overturned, reveal what
was hidden, point out the way to one who was lost, or hold up an oil lamp in
the dark so that those with eyes could see forms, so too the Blessed One has
made the Dhamma clear in many ways.

I go for refuge to the Buddha, to the Dhamma, and to the Saṅgha of bhikkhus.
May the Blessed One remember me as a lay follower gone for refuge from this
day for life.

I transgressed, bhante. It was foolish, stupid, and unwholesome of me to kill
my father, a just and principled king, for the sake of sovereignty. May the
Blessed One accept that transgression as a transgression, so that I may show
restraint in the future."

"Indeed, great king, you transgressed. It was foolish, stupid, and
unwholesome of you to kill your father, a just and principled king, for the
sake of sovereignty.

But since you have seen your transgression as a transgression and have dealt
with it in line with the Dhamma, I accept it.

For growth in the discipline of the noble ones is this: to see a
transgression as a transgression, to deal with it in line with the Dhamma, and
to undertake restraint for the future."

Then the king said:

"Now, bhante, we will go. We have many duties and much to do."

"Go then, great king, when you think it is time."

Then King Ajātasattu, delighted and approving of what the Buddha had said,
rose from his seat, bowed to the Buddha, and respectfully circled him with
his right side before leaving.

### The Buddha's Final Remark

Soon after the king had left, the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus:

"Broken is the king, bhikkhus. Ruined is the king. If he had not killed his
father, a just and principled king, then in that very seat the dustless,
stainless Dhamma-eye would have arisen in him."

That is what the Buddha said. The bhikkhus were glad and welcomed what the
Buddha had said.
