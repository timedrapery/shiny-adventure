# MN 64 Translation Notes

This document is the companion rationale for
[MN 64: Mahāmālukya Sutta](mn64-mahamalukya-sutta.md). The main translation is
the primary study surface; this file records debated translation choices,
source-audit calls, and edition notes that govern it.

## Source Base

- Primary source: the Pali text of MN 64 as segmented in SuttaCentral's Bilara
  data.
- Control surface: the repository's current term policy, especially
  `saṃyojana`, `orambhāgiya-saṃyojana`, `anusaya`, `sakkāyadiṭṭhi`,
  `vicikicchā`, `sīlabbata-parāmāsa`, `kāmacchanda`, `kāma-rāga`, `byāpāda`,
  `nissaraṇa`, `yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti`, `jhāna`, `vitakka`, `vicāra`, `pīti`,
  `sukha`, `rūpa`, `vedanā`, `saññā`, `saṅkhārā`, `viññāṇa`, `anicca`,
  `dukkha`, `anattā`, `suññatā`, `amata`, `upadhi`, `paṭinissagga`,
  `taṇhā`, `khaya`, `virāga`, `nirodha`, `nibbāna`, `āsava`, `cetovimutti`,
  `paññāvimutti`, and the generated bondage-residue, jhāna, five-heaps,
  consummation-interface, and three-marks control sheets.
- Working method: the discourse was governed as one argument about how lower
  fetters become active and how their underlying tendencies are abandoned,
  rather than as a loose collection of similes and meditative attainments.

## Governing Decisions

- `saṃyojana` remains `fetter`.
- `pañcorambhāgiyāni saṃyojanāni` is rendered `the five lower fetters`.
- `anusaya` remains `underlying tendency`, preserving the latent force that the
  infant simile is designed to expose.
- Mālukyaputta's initial `kāmacchanda` is rendered `sensual distraction`, while
  the Buddha's later `kāma-rāga` is rendered `passion for sensuality`. The
  distinction is kept because the source itself shifts terms.
- `byāpāda` is rendered `aversive distraction` in both the list and the
  later lower-fetter analysis. This keeps the text aligned with the live
  `byāpāda` record while preserving the fetter context in the notes.
- The infant-simile tendency lines render direct compounds as:
  `underlying tendency toward identity view`, `underlying tendency toward
  doubt`, `underlying tendency toward grasping at rules and observances`,
  `underlying tendency toward sensual passion`, and `underlying tendency toward
  aversive distraction`.
- `nissaraṇaṃ yathābhūtaṃ pajānāti` is rendered `discerns its escape as it has
  come to be`, preserving both the escape-family and the processive
  yathābhūta language.
- `jhāna` remains `mental theme`, with the first four members coordinated with
  the existing DN 2, MN 19, MN 44, MN 99, and MN 141 surfaces.
- `ākāsānañcāyatana`, `viññāṇañcāyatana`, and `ākiñcaññāyatana` are rendered
  `sphere of boundless space`, `sphere of boundless knowing`, and `sphere of
  nothingness`.
- `amatā dhātu` is rendered `element of the deathless`, so `amata` remains
  distinct from `nibbāna` and from `asaṅkhata`.
- The consummation formula is rendered from the existing local controls:
  `the calming of all constructions, the relinquishment of all supports, the
  wearing away of ignorant wanting, fading, quenching, nibbāna`.
- `dhammarāga` and `dhammanandī` are rendered `passion for the Dhamma` and
  `relishing in the Dhamma` as document-level renderings. No new term record
  is added because this surface does not yet prove a repeated family need.
- `cetovimutti` and `paññāvimutti` are rendered `release of mind` and `release
  through discernment`.

## Re-audit Calls

- The translation keeps `Bhante` in the replies to the Buddha, matching the
  repo's governed dialogue voice.
- The Buddha's rebuke of Mālukyaputta is not treated as a rejection of the
  lower-fetter list itself. The problem is that a merely active-state list
  leaves the infant-simile challenge unanswered unless underlying tendencies
  are distinguished from manifest fetters.
- `vicikicchā` is `uncertain distraction` when it is active and overwhelming
  the feeling mind. The compound `vicikicchānusaya` is rendered with the
  existing minor-entry wording `underlying tendency toward doubt`.
- `kāmacchanda` appears only in Mālukyaputta's opening list and the infant
  challenge. The main analysis uses `kāma-rāga`, so the translation shifts
  there to `passion for sensuality`.
- `thāmagata appaṭivinīta` is rendered `firmly established, not brought under
  training`. The point is that the arisen state has strength and has not been
  disciplined or removed.
- The learned noble disciple's refrain uses `abandoned together with its
  underlying tendency` for `sānusayā pahīyati`, because the discourse is about
  abandoning both the active manifestation and the latent residue.
- The tree simile uses `bark`, `sapwood`, and `heartwood`. It supports the
  practical point that lower fetters are not reached by skipping the path that
  cuts through the outer layers first.
- The Ganges simile renders `cittaṃ pakkhandati pasīdati santiṭṭhati
  vimuccati` as the feeling mind leaping forward, becoming confident,
  settling, and being released. This keeps four movements audible rather than
  flattening the line into generic faith.
- `upadhivivekā` is rendered `with seclusion from supports`. This keeps the
  connection to the `upadhi` headword while avoiding a premature metaphysical
  expansion.
- `sabbaso kāyaduṭṭhullānaṃ paṭippassaddhiyā` is rendered `with the complete
  calming of bodily heaviness`. This is a local readability choice because no
  current term record requires a broader policy change.
- The first mental-theme analysis keeps the five experiential categories in
  their broad process forms: form, felt experience, recognition, putting
  things together, and knowing.
- The formless-sphere analyses omit form from the contemplated categories,
  following the root's shift from `rūpagataṃ ... viññāṇagataṃ` to
  `vedanāgataṃ ... viññāṇagataṃ`.
- The root abbreviates the repeated mental-theme and formless-sphere analyses
  with ellipses. The translation expands only enough to keep the argument
  readable while making clear that the same deathless-element turn and the same
  two outcomes govern each attainment.
- The formless sequence stops at the sphere of nothingness because the Pali
  source stops there in this discourse.
- `dhammarāga` and `dhammanandī` are kept positive-but-still-conditioned. The
  line explains non-returning when the outflows have not yet worn away; it does
  not make passion or relishing into the final release itself.
- Ānanda's final question keeps the pair `release of mind` and `release
  through discernment` visible. The Buddha's answer attributes the difference
  to faculties, not to two unrelated paths.

## Practice Clarifications

- MN 64 is a key surface for keeping `anusaya` and `saṃyojana` apart. A fetter
  can be actively overwhelming the feeling mind, while an underlying tendency
  can lie underneath even when the active object has not yet appeared.
- The infant simile matters because it blocks a shallow reading in which only
  currently manifest states count. The child does not yet have identity,
  dhammas, rules, sensualities, or beings in the relevant practical sense, but
  tendencies still lie underneath.
- The learned noble disciple is not merely suppressing lower fetters. The
  refrain says the state is abandoned together with its underlying tendency.
- The path cannot be skipped. The tree simile and the Ganges simile both make
  the point that knowing, seeing, and abandoning the lower fetters requires the
  path and way of practice.
- The mental themes and formless spheres are not treated as final destinations.
  In each case the bhikkhu turns the feeling mind back from whatever dhammas
  are present there and brings it to the element of the deathless.
- The analysis of form, felt experience, recognition, putting things together,
  and knowing keeps refined meditative experience inside the same
  impermanence, dissatisfaction, emptiness, and not-self scrutiny as ordinary
  experience.
- The fallback outcome is still high but not final arahantship: because of
  passion for the Dhamma and relishing in the Dhamma, one may become a
  non-returner through the complete wearing away of the five lower fetters.

## Editorial Presentation

- The main translation file uses section headings so the rebuke, similes, path
  analysis, and final distinction between release of mind and release through
  discernment can function as a study text.
- Those headings are editorial aids, not claims that such headings stand in the
  root text itself.
- The repeated lower-fetter analysis is translated in full because it is the
  doctrinal center of the discourse.
- The repeated practice formula is translated in full once and then compressed
  where the Pali itself abbreviates with ellipses.

## Edition Status

- This companion now supports the first stable study edition of the repo's MN
  64 translation surface.
- Future revisions should be driven by source-audit gains, term-policy
  expansion, or real readability improvements, not routine wording churn.
