# SN 22.89: Khemaka Sutta

  as a single Markdown document.

  Bilara data.
- House choices that most affect this sutta: `bhikkhu` remains untranslated;
  `pañcupādānakkhandhā` is rendered `five clung-to heaps`; `rūpa` is rendered
  `form`; `vedanā` is rendered `felt experience`; `saññā` is rendered
  `recognition`; `saṅkhārā` in heap contexts is rendered
  `putting-together activities`; `viññāṇa` is rendered `knowing`; `atta` is
  rendered `self`; `attaniya` is rendered `belonging to self`; `asmimāna` is
  rendered `conceit 'I am'`; `māna` is rendered `conceit`; `chanda` is rendered
  `desire`; `anusaya` is rendered `underlying tendency`; `saṃyojana` is
  rendered `fetter`; `orambhāgiya-saṃyojana` is rendered `lower fetter`;
  `āsava` is rendered `outflow`; and `anupādāya` is rendered `through not
  taking anything personally`.
- This edition treats SN 22.89 as a control passage for distinguishing explicit
  self-view in relation to the heaps from the subtler residual conceit "I am."

## Translation

### The Setting

At one time several senior bhikkhus were staying near Kosambī, in Ghosita's
monastery.

At that time Venerable Khemaka was staying in the Jujube Grove, sick,
suffering, and gravely ill.

Then, in the evening, the senior bhikkhus emerged from retreat and addressed
Venerable Dāsaka:

"Come, friend Dāsaka, go to the bhikkhu Khemaka. After you have gone to him,
say this:

'Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

"We hope it is bearable for you, friend. We hope you are getting by. We hope
your painful felt experiences are receding, not increasing, and that their
receding is evident, not their increasing."'"

"Yes, friends," Venerable Dāsaka replied to the senior bhikkhus.

He went to Venerable Khemaka and said this:

"Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

'We hope it is bearable for you. We hope you are getting by. We hope your
painful felt experiences are receding, not increasing, and that their receding
is evident, not their increasing.'"

"Friend, it is not bearable for me. I am not getting by. My painful felt
experiences are increasing, not receding, and their increasing is evident, not
their receding."

### The Question About Self

Then Venerable Dāsaka went to the senior bhikkhus and said this:

"Friends, the bhikkhu Khemaka says this:

'It is not bearable for me. I am not getting by. My painful felt experiences
are increasing, not receding, and their increasing is evident, not their
receding.'"

"Come, friend Dāsaka, go to the bhikkhu Khemaka. After you have gone to him,
say this:

'Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

"Friend, the Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps:

- the form clung-to heap
- the felt-experience clung-to heap
- the recognition clung-to heap
- the putting-together-activities clung-to heap
- the knowing clung-to heap

Does Venerable Khemaka regard anything among these five clung-to heaps as self
or as belonging to self?"'"

"Yes, friends," Venerable Dāsaka replied to the senior bhikkhus.

He went to Venerable Khemaka and relayed the message.

Venerable Khemaka replied:

"Friend, the Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps:

- the form clung-to heap
- the felt-experience clung-to heap
- the recognition clung-to heap
- the putting-together-activities clung-to heap
- the knowing clung-to heap

I do not regard anything among these five clung-to heaps as self or as
belonging to self."

Then Venerable Dāsaka went to the senior bhikkhus and said this:

"Friends, the bhikkhu Khemaka says this:

'The Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps: the form clung-to heap, the
felt-experience clung-to heap, the recognition clung-to heap, the
putting-together-activities clung-to heap, and the knowing clung-to heap.

I do not regard anything among these five clung-to heaps as self or as
belonging to self.'"

### Not Yet an Arahant

"Come, friend Dāsaka, go to the bhikkhu Khemaka. After you have gone to him,
say this:

'Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

"Friend, the Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps: the form clung-to
heap, the felt-experience clung-to heap, the recognition clung-to heap, the
putting-together-activities clung-to heap, and the knowing clung-to heap.

If Venerable Khemaka does not regard anything among these five clung-to heaps
as self or as belonging to self, then Venerable Khemaka is an arahant whose
outflows are worn away."'"

"Yes, friends," Venerable Dāsaka replied to the senior bhikkhus.

He went to Venerable Khemaka and relayed the message.

Venerable Khemaka replied:

"Friend, the Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps: the form clung-to
heap, the felt-experience clung-to heap, the recognition clung-to heap, the
putting-together-activities clung-to heap, and the knowing clung-to heap.

I do not regard anything among these five clung-to heaps as self or as
belonging to self, but I am not an arahant whose outflows are worn away.

Still, among these five clung-to heaps, 'I am' is found in me, but I do not
regard anything as 'I am this.'"

Then Venerable Dāsaka went to the senior bhikkhus and said this:

"Friends, the bhikkhu Khemaka says this:

'The Buddha has taught these five clung-to heaps: the form clung-to heap, the
felt-experience clung-to heap, the recognition clung-to heap, the
putting-together-activities clung-to heap, and the knowing clung-to heap.

I do not regard anything among these five clung-to heaps as self or as
belonging to self, but I am not an arahant whose outflows are worn away.

Still, among these five clung-to heaps, "I am" is found in me, but I do not
regard anything as "I am this."'"

### Enough Running Back and Forth

"Come, friend Dāsaka, go to the bhikkhu Khemaka. After you have gone to him,
say this:

'Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

"Friend Khemaka, when you say this, 'I am,' what do you say is 'I am'?

Do you say form is 'I am,' or apart from form is 'I am'?

Do you say felt experience is 'I am,' or apart from felt experience is 'I am'?

Do you say recognition is 'I am,' or apart from recognition is 'I am'?

Do you say putting-together activities are 'I am,' or apart from
putting-together activities is 'I am'?

Do you say knowing is 'I am,' or apart from knowing is 'I am'?

When you say this, 'I am,' what do you say is 'I am'?"'"

"Yes, friends," Venerable Dāsaka replied to the senior bhikkhus.

He went to Venerable Khemaka and said:

"Friend Khemaka, the senior bhikkhus say this:

'Friend Khemaka, when you say this, "I am," what do you say is "I am"?

Do you say form is "I am," or apart from form is "I am"?

Do you say felt experience is "I am," or apart from felt experience is
"I am"?

Do you say recognition is "I am," or apart from recognition is "I am"?

Do you say putting-together activities are "I am," or apart from
putting-together activities is "I am"?

Do you say knowing is "I am," or apart from knowing is "I am"?

When you say this, "I am," what do you say is "I am"?'"

"Enough, friend Dāsaka. What is the point of this running back and forth?

Bring me my staff, friend. I will go to the senior bhikkhus myself."

### Khemaka Comes to the Senior Bhikkhus

Then Venerable Khemaka, leaning on a staff, went to the senior bhikkhus. After
he had gone to them, he exchanged greetings with them.

When the greetings and polite conversation were complete, he sat to one side.
Sitting to one side, the senior bhikkhus said this to Venerable Khemaka:

"Friend Khemaka, when you say this, 'I am,' what do you say is 'I am'?

Do you say form is 'I am,' or apart from form is 'I am'?

Do you say felt experience is 'I am,' or apart from felt experience is 'I am'?

Do you say recognition is 'I am,' or apart from recognition is 'I am'?

Do you say putting-together activities are 'I am,' or apart from
putting-together activities is 'I am'?

Do you say knowing is 'I am,' or apart from knowing is 'I am'?

When you say this, 'I am,' what do you say is 'I am'?"

"Friends, I do not say form is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from form is 'I am.'

I do not say felt experience is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from felt
experience is 'I am.'

I do not say recognition is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from recognition is
'I am.'

I do not say putting-together activities are 'I am,' nor do I say apart from
putting-together activities is 'I am.'

I do not say knowing is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from knowing is 'I am.'

Still, among these five clung-to heaps, 'I am' is found in me, but I do not
regard anything as 'I am this.'

### The Flower Scent Simile

"Suppose, friends, there were the scent of a blue water lily, a lotus, or a
white lotus.

If someone were to say, 'It is the scent of the petal,' or 'It is the scent of
the color,' or 'It is the scent of the pollen,' would they be speaking
correctly?"

"No, friend."

"Then, friends, how would one answer correctly?"

"Friend, one would answer correctly by saying, 'It is the scent of the
flower.'"

"In the same way, friends, I do not say form is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from
form is 'I am.'

I do not say felt experience is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from felt
experience is 'I am.'

I do not say recognition is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from recognition is
'I am.'

I do not say putting-together activities are 'I am,' nor do I say apart from
putting-together activities is 'I am.'

I do not say knowing is 'I am,' nor do I say apart from knowing is 'I am.'

Still, among these five clung-to heaps, 'I am' is found in me, but I do not
regard anything as 'I am this.'

### The Lingering Residue

"Friends, although a noble disciple has abandoned the five lower fetters, still
there remains, following along among the five clung-to heaps, a conceit
'I am,' a desire 'I am,' and an underlying tendency 'I am' that has not been
uprooted.

At a later time, one remains observing arising and passing away in the five
clung-to heaps:

'Such is form. Such is the arising of form. Such is the vanishing of form.

Such is felt experience. Such is the arising of felt experience. Such is the
vanishing of felt experience.

Such is recognition. Such is the arising of recognition. Such is the
vanishing of recognition.

Such are putting-together activities. Such is the arising of putting-together
activities. Such is the vanishing of putting-together activities.

Such is knowing. Such is the arising of knowing. Such is the vanishing of
knowing.'

As one remains observing arising and passing away in these five clung-to heaps,
whatever remains, following along among the five clung-to heaps, as a conceit
'I am,' a desire 'I am,' and an underlying tendency 'I am' that has not been
uprooted - that too goes to uprooting.

### The Cloth Scent Simile

"Suppose, friends, there were a cloth that was stained and soiled. Its owners
would hand it over to a washer.

The washer would knead it with salt, lye, or cow dung, and rinse it in clear
water.

Although the cloth would be clean and bright, still the smell of salt, lye, or
cow dung would remain, following along with it, not yet uprooted.

The washer would give it back to its owners. The owners would put it in a
fragrant chest.

Whatever smell of salt, lye, or cow dung had remained, following along with it,
not yet uprooted - that too would go to uprooting.

"In the same way, friends, although a noble disciple has abandoned the five
lower fetters, still there remains, following along among the five clung-to
heaps, a conceit 'I am,' a desire 'I am,' and an underlying tendency 'I am'
that has not been uprooted.

At a later time, one remains observing arising and passing away in the five
clung-to heaps:

'Such is form. Such is the arising of form. Such is the vanishing of form.

Such is felt experience. Such is the arising of felt experience. Such is the
vanishing of felt experience.

Such is recognition. Such is the arising of recognition. Such is the
vanishing of recognition.

Such are putting-together activities. Such is the arising of putting-together
activities. Such is the vanishing of putting-together activities.

Such is knowing. Such is the arising of knowing. Such is the vanishing of
knowing.'

As one remains observing arising and passing away in these five clung-to heaps,
whatever remains, following along among the five clung-to heaps, as a conceit
'I am,' a desire 'I am,' and an underlying tendency 'I am' that has not been
uprooted - that too goes to uprooting."

### The Result

When this had been said, the senior bhikkhus said this to Venerable Khemaka:

"We did not question Venerable Khemaka out of a wish to trouble him.

But Venerable Khemaka is able to explain, teach, designate, establish, open,
analyze, and make clear the Buddha's instruction in detail.

And now Venerable Khemaka has explained, taught, designated, established,
opened, analyzed, and made clear the Buddha's instruction in detail."

That is what Venerable Khemaka said.

The senior bhikkhus were satisfied and welcomed what Venerable Khemaka had
said.

And while this exposition was being spoken, the minds of about sixty senior
bhikkhus, and the mind of Venerable Khemaka, were released from the outflows
through not taking anything personally.
