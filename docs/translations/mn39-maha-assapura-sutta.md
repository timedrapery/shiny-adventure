# MN 39: Mahā-Assapura Sutta

## Editorial Note

  `hirī-ottappa` is rendered `conscience and concern`; `sati` is rendered

## Translation

### The Setting

So I have heard.

At one time the Blessed One was staying in the land of the Aṅgas, near the
Aṅgan town named Assapura. There the Blessed One addressed the bhikkhus:
"Bhikkhus."

"Bhante," those bhikkhus replied.

The Blessed One said this:

### What Makes a True Recluse

"Bhikkhus, people recognize you as recluses. And when asked what you are, you
claim to be recluses. Given this recognition and this claim, you should train
like this: 'We will undertake and follow the qualities that make one a recluse
and a brahmin. In this way our recognition will be accurate and our claim
true. The services of those whose robes, alms-food, lodgings, medicines, and
supplies for the sick we enjoy will be very fruitful and beneficial for them.
And our going forth will not be without fruit — it will be fruitful and
worthwhile.'

So you should train, bhikkhus.

And what are the qualities that make one a recluse and a brahmin?

You should train like this: 'We will be possessed of conscience and concern.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern.
That is enough — enough has been done; the recluse's goal has been reached;
there is nothing more for us to do.' And you might settle for just that much.

I declare to you, bhikkhus, I announce to you: 'You who seek the recluse's
goal — do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still more to do.'

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'Our bodily conduct will be pure — open, disclosed,
neither inconsistent nor concealed. And on account of our pure bodily conduct
we will neither praise ourselves nor look down on others.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily conduct is pure. That is enough …' And you might settle for just
that much.

I declare to you, bhikkhus, I announce to you: 'You who seek the recluse's
goal — do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still more to do.'

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'Our verbal conduct will be pure — open, disclosed,
neither inconsistent nor concealed. And on account of our pure verbal conduct
we will neither praise ourselves nor look down on others.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily conduct is pure; our verbal conduct is pure. That is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'Our mental conduct will be pure — open, disclosed,
neither inconsistent nor concealed. And on account of our pure mental conduct
we will neither praise ourselves nor look down on others.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure. That is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'Our livelihood will be pure — open, disclosed,
neither inconsistent nor concealed. And on account of our pure livelihood we
will neither praise ourselves nor look down on others.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure; our livelihood is pure. That
is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'We will be guarded at the faculty doors. On
seeing a sight with the eye, we will not grasp at the overall sign or at its
details. Since living with the eye-faculty unguarded would let bad, unskillful
qualities of covetousness and displeasure overwhelm us, we will practice
restraint, guard the eye-faculty, and reach restraint of the eye-faculty.

On hearing a sound with the ear …

On smelling a smell with the nose …

On tasting a flavor with the tongue …

On touching a contact with the body …

On knowing an idea with the mind, we will not grasp at the overall sign or at
its details. Since living with the mind-faculty unguarded would let bad,
unskillful qualities of covetousness and displeasure overwhelm us, we will
practice restraint, guard the mind-faculty, and reach restraint of the
mind-faculty.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure; our livelihood is pure; we are
guarded at the faculty doors. That is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'We will know measure in eating. Reflecting wisely,
we will take food — not for fun, not for indulgence, not for adornment, not
for decoration, but only to sustain this body, to keep it going, to prevent
harm, and to support the spiritual life. In this way we will put an end to the
old felt experience and not give rise to a new one, and we will live in health,
blamelessness, and ease.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure; our livelihood is pure; we are
guarded at the faculty doors; we know measure in eating. That is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'We will be devoted to wakefulness. During the
day, by walking and sitting, we will purify the feeling mind from obstructing
qualities. In the first watch of the night, by walking and sitting, we will
purify the feeling mind from obstructing qualities. In the middle watch of the
night, we will lie down on the right side in the lion's posture, one foot on
the other, with remembering and clear knowing, intending to get up. In the
last watch of the night, getting up and walking and sitting, we will purify
the feeling mind from obstructing qualities.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure; our livelihood is pure; we are
guarded at the faculty doors; we know measure in eating; we are devoted to
wakefulness. That is enough …'

I declare to you: do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still
more to do.

And what more is there to do?

You should train like this: 'We will be possessed of remembering and clear
knowing. We will act with clear knowing when going out and coming back; when
looking ahead and looking aside; when bending and extending; when bearing the
outer robe, bowl, and robes; when eating, drinking, chewing, and tasting;
when using the toilet; when walking, standing, sitting, lying down, waking,
speaking, and keeping silent.'

But bhikkhus, you might think: 'We are possessed of conscience and concern;
our bodily, verbal, and mental conduct is pure; our livelihood is pure; we are
guarded at the faculty doors; we know measure in eating; we are devoted to
wakefulness; we are possessed of remembering and clear knowing. That is
enough — enough has been done; the recluse's goal has been reached; there is
nothing more for us to do.' And you might settle for just that much.

I declare to you, bhikkhus, I announce to you: 'You who seek the recluse's
goal — do not let the recluse's goal decline while there is still more to do.'

### Settling Into Seclusion

And what more is there to do?

Here, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu frequents a secluded lodging — a wilderness, the
root of a tree, a hillside, a ravine, a mountain cave, a charnel ground, a
forest, the open air, a heap of straw.

After returning from the alms-round following the meal, they sit down, cross
their legs, set their body upright, and establish remembering before them.

### Abandoning the Five Distractions

Giving up covetousness for the world, they remain with a feeling mind free of
covetousness, and purify the feeling mind of covetousness.

Giving up ill will and malice, they remain with a feeling mind free of ill
will, full of compassion toward all living beings, and purify the feeling mind
of ill will.

Giving up dull distraction, they remain with the feeling mind free of dull
distraction, with awareness of light, with remembering and clear knowing, and
purify the feeling mind of dull distraction.

Giving up agitated distraction, they remain without agitation, the feeling
mind inwardly stilled, and purify the feeling mind of agitated distraction.

Giving up uncertain distraction, they remain having crossed over uncertainty,
with no wavering over skillful qualities, and purify the feeling mind of
uncertain distraction.

### Five Similes for the Distractions

Bhikkhus, suppose a man were to take out a loan and apply himself to work, and
his work were to succeed. He would pay off his original debts and have enough
left over to support a partner.

He would think: 'Before, I took out a loan and applied myself to work; my work
succeeded. I paid off my original debts and have enough left over to support
a partner.'

From that he would gain gladness and arrive at joy.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person were sick, in pain, gravely ill. Their food would
not agree with them and their body would have no strength. After a time they
would recover from that illness; their food would agree with them; their body
would have strength.

They would think: 'Before, I was sick, in pain, gravely ill; my food did not
agree with me and my body had no strength. Now I have recovered; my food
agrees with me; my body has strength.'

From that they would gain gladness and arrive at joy.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person were imprisoned. After a time they were released
from prison safely and without loss of property.

They would think: 'Before, I was imprisoned. Now I have been released safely
and without loss of property.'

From that they would gain gladness and arrive at joy.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person were a slave — not their own master, bound to
another, unable to go where they wish. After a time they were freed from that
slavery, their own master, not bound to another, free to go where they wish.

They would think: 'Before, I was a slave — not my own master, bound to
another, unable to go where I wished. Now I am freed — my own master, not
bound to another, free to go where I wish.'

From that they would gain gladness and arrive at joy.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person with wealth and property were to travel along a
desert road. After a time they crossed the desert safely and without loss of
property.

They would think: 'Before, I had wealth and property and traveled along a
desert road. Now I have crossed the desert safely and without loss of
property.'

From that they would gain gladness and arrive at joy.

In the same way, bhikkhus, as long as a bhikkhu sees these five distractions
as not given up within themselves, they regard them as a debt, an illness, a
prison, slavery, and a desert road.

But when a bhikkhu sees these five distractions as given up within themselves,
they regard this as freedom from debt, good health, release from prison,
freedom, and safe ground.

### The Four Mental Themes

Giving up these five distractions — corruptions of the feeling mind that
weaken discernment — secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from
unskillful qualities, they enter and remain in the first mental theme: with
thinking and pondering, with delight and happiness born of seclusion.

They drench, saturate, fill, and spread this very body with delight and
happiness born of seclusion, so that no part of the whole body goes
undrenched with delight and happiness born of seclusion.

Bhikkhus, suppose a skilled bathhouse attendant or their apprentice were to
scatter bath powder into a bronze basin and knead it with water little by
little until the powder ball was thoroughly saturated with moisture — inside
and out, soaked through — yet nothing dripped off.

In the same way, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu drenches, saturates, fills, and spreads
this very body with delight and happiness born of seclusion, so that no part
of the whole body goes undrenched with delight and happiness born of seclusion.

Furthermore, bhikkhus, with the stilling of thinking and pondering, with
internal clarity, the feeling mind unified, without thinking and pondering, a
bhikkhu enters and remains in the second mental theme: with delight and
happiness born of mental composure.

They drench, saturate, fill, and spread this very body with delight and
happiness born of mental composure, so that no part of the whole body goes
undrenched with delight and happiness born of mental composure.

Bhikkhus, suppose there were a lake with spring water welling up inside it,
with no inlet from the east, west, north, or south, and the sky giving no
rainfall from time to time. A cool stream of water would well up from within
that lake and drench, saturate, fill, and spread throughout the lake, so that
no part of the whole lake went undrenched with cool water.

In the same way, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu drenches, saturates, fills, and spreads
this very body with delight and happiness born of mental composure, so that
no part of the whole body goes undrenched with delight and happiness born of
mental composure.

Furthermore, bhikkhus, with the fading of delight, a bhikkhu remains in
dynamic balance, with remembering and clear knowing, and experiences with the
body the happiness of which the noble ones say: 'One who has dynamic balance
and remembers lives in happiness.' They enter and remain in the third mental
theme.

They drench, saturate, fill, and spread this very body with happiness free of
delight, so that no part of the whole body goes undrenched with happiness free
of delight.

Bhikkhus, suppose in a pond of blue lotuses, or pink or white lotuses, some
lotuses were born and grew in the water, rose no higher than the water, and
were nourished while submerged. From tip to root they would be drenched,
saturated, filled, and permeated with cool water, so that no part of any lotus
went undrenched with cool water.

In the same way, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu drenches, saturates, fills, and spreads
this very body with happiness free of delight, so that no part of the whole
body goes undrenched with happiness free of delight.

Furthermore, bhikkhus, with the abandoning of pleasure and pain, and with the
earlier disappearance of gladness and sadness, a bhikkhu enters and remains in
the fourth mental theme: neither painful nor pleasant, with pure dynamic
balance and remembering.

They sit suffusing this very body with a pure, bright feeling mind, so that no
part of the whole body goes unsuffused with a pure, bright feeling mind.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person were to sit wrapped from head to foot in white
cloth, so that no part of the whole body went unwrapped in white cloth.

In the same way, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu sits suffusing this very body with a
pure, bright feeling mind, so that no part of the whole body goes unsuffused
with a pure, bright feeling mind.

### Three Higher Knowledges

With the feeling mind so concentrated, purified, bright, without blemish, free
from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable, they direct it
toward the knowledge of remembering past lives.

They remember many kinds of past lives in their various modes and details.

Bhikkhus, suppose a person were to leave their home village, go to another
village, and from that village go to yet another village — and then return to
their home village. They would think: 'I left my home village and went to that
village. There I stood like this, sat like that, spoke like this, kept silent
like that. From that village I went to yet another village. There too I stood
like this, sat like that, spoke like this, kept silent like that. And from
that village I returned to my home village.'

In the same way, bhikkhus, a bhikkhu remembers many kinds of past lives in
their various modes and details.

With the feeling mind so concentrated, purified, bright, without blemish, free
from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable, they direct it
toward the knowledge of the death and rebirth of beings.

With the divine eye — purified, beyond the human range — they see beings
passing away and being reborn, lower and higher, beautiful and ugly, fortunate
and unfortunate, and they understand how beings pass on according to their
actions.

Bhikkhus, suppose there were two houses with doors, and a person with good
eyes standing between them could see people entering and leaving a house,
walking back and forth.

In the same way, with the divine eye — purified, beyond the human range — a
bhikkhu sees beings passing away and being reborn, lower and higher, beautiful
and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate, and understands how beings pass on
according to their actions.

With the feeling mind so concentrated, purified, bright, without blemish, free
from corruption, pliable, workable, steady, and imperturbable, they direct it
toward the knowledge of the destruction of outflows.

They truly understand: 'This is dissatisfaction.' They truly understand: 'This
is the arising of dissatisfaction.' They truly understand: 'This is the
cessation of dissatisfaction.' They truly understand: 'This is the path
leading to the cessation of dissatisfaction.'

They truly understand: 'These are the outflows.' They truly understand: 'This
is the arising of outflows.' They truly understand: 'This is the cessation of
outflows.' They truly understand: 'This is the path leading to the cessation
of outflows.'

Knowing and seeing in this way, the feeling mind is released from the outflow
of sensual desire, released from the outflow of becoming, released from the
outflow of ignorance.

When released, the knowledge arises: 'Released.' They understand: 'Birth is
finished, the spiritual life has been lived, what had to be done has been done,
there is nothing further for this.'

Bhikkhus, suppose in a mountain glen there were a lake, clear, limpid, and
undisturbed. A person with good eyes standing on the bank would see oysters
and shells, pebbles and gravel, and schools of fish moving about or keeping
still.

They would think: 'This lake is clear, limpid, and undisturbed. There are the
oysters and shells, pebbles and gravel, and schools of fish moving about or
keeping still.'

In the same way, a bhikkhu truly understands: 'This is dissatisfaction … this
is the path leading to the cessation of dissatisfaction.' They truly
understand: 'These are the outflows … there is nothing further for this.'

### Seven Titles Explained

This bhikkhu, bhikkhus, is called a recluse, a brahmin, a bathed one, a
knowledge-master, one cleansed, a noble one, a perfected one.

And how, bhikkhus, is a bhikkhu a recluse? Their bad, unskillful qualities —
corrupting, leading to further becoming, accompanied by trouble, resulting in
painful fruit, causing future birth, ageing, and death — have been calmed.
That is how a bhikkhu is a recluse.

And how is a bhikkhu a brahmin? Those same bad, unskillful qualities have been
expelled. That is how a bhikkhu is a brahmin.

And how is a bhikkhu a bathed one? Those same bad, unskillful qualities have
been bathed away. That is how a bhikkhu is a bathed one.

And how is a bhikkhu a knowledge-master? Those same bad, unskillful qualities
have been fully known. That is how a bhikkhu is a knowledge-master.

And how is a bhikkhu one cleansed? Those same bad, unskillful qualities have
been scoured off. That is how a bhikkhu is one cleansed.

And how is a bhikkhu a noble one? Those same bad, unskillful qualities are
kept far away. That is how a bhikkhu is a noble one.

And how is a bhikkhu a perfected one? Those same bad, unskillful qualities —
corrupting, leading to further becoming, accompanied by trouble, resulting in
painful fruit, causing future birth, ageing, and death — are kept impeccably
far away. That is how a bhikkhu is a perfected one."

That is what the Blessed One said. Satisfied, those bhikkhus approved of the
Blessed One's words.

The Greater Discourse at Assapura is finished.
