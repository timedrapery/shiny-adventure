## Translation

### The Setting

So I heard.

At one time the Buddha was staying near Bārāṇasī, at Isipatana, in the Deer
Park.

There the Buddha addressed the group of five bhikkhus: "Bhikkhus."

"Bhante," they replied.

The Buddha said this:

### The Five Heaps Are Not-Self

"Bhikkhus, form is not-self.

For if form were self, form would not lead to affliction, and it would be
possible to say of form: 'May my form be like this; may my form not be like
that.'

But because form is not-self, form leads to affliction, and it is not
possible to say of form: 'May my form be like this; may my form not be like
that.'

Felt experience is not-self.

For if felt experience were self, felt experience would not lead to
affliction, and it would be possible to say of felt experience: 'May my felt
experience be like this; may my felt experience not be like that.'

But because felt experience is not-self, felt experience leads to affliction,
and it is not possible to say of felt experience: 'May my felt experience be
like this; may my felt experience not be like that.'

Recognition is not-self.

For if recognition were self, recognition would not lead to affliction, and it
would be possible to say of recognition: 'May my recognition be like this; may
my recognition not be like that.'

But because recognition is not-self, recognition leads to affliction, and it
is not possible to say of recognition: 'May my recognition be like this; may
my recognition not be like that.'

Putting-together activities are not-self.

For if putting-together activities were self, putting-together activities
would not lead to affliction, and it would be possible to say of
putting-together activities: 'May my putting-together activities be like this;
may my putting-together activities not be like that.'

But because putting-together activities are not-self, putting-together
activities lead to affliction, and it is not possible to say of
putting-together activities: 'May my putting-together activities be like this;
may my putting-together activities not be like that.'

Knowing is not-self.

For if knowing were self, knowing would not lead to affliction, and it would
be possible to say of knowing: 'May my knowing be like this; may my knowing
not be like that.'

But because knowing is not-self, knowing leads to affliction, and it is not
possible to say of knowing: 'May my knowing be like this; may my knowing not
be like that.'"

### Impermanence, Dissatisfactoriness, And Not-Self

"What do you think, bhikkhus? Is form permanent or impermanent?"

"Impermanent, bhante."

"And what is impermanent: is it dissatisfaction or happiness?"

"Dissatisfaction, bhante."

"And what is impermanent, dissatisfactory, and subject to change: is it fit to
regard it like this: 'This is mine, I am this, this is my self'?"

"No, bhante."

"Is felt experience permanent or impermanent?"

"Impermanent, bhante."

"And what is impermanent: is it dissatisfaction or happiness?"

"Dissatisfaction, bhante."

"And what is impermanent, dissatisfactory, and subject to change: is it fit to
regard it like this: 'This is mine, I am this, this is my self'?"

"No, bhante."

"Is recognition permanent or impermanent?"

"Impermanent, bhante."

"And what is impermanent: is it dissatisfaction or happiness?"

"Dissatisfaction, bhante."

"And what is impermanent, dissatisfactory, and subject to change: is it fit to
regard it like this: 'This is mine, I am this, this is my self'?"

"No, bhante."

"Are putting-together activities permanent or impermanent?"

"Impermanent, bhante."

"And what is impermanent: is it dissatisfaction or happiness?"

"Dissatisfaction, bhante."

"And what is impermanent, dissatisfactory, and subject to change: is it fit to
regard it like this: 'This is mine, I am this, this is my self'?"

"No, bhante."

"Is knowing permanent or impermanent?"

"Impermanent, bhante."

"And what is impermanent: is it dissatisfaction or happiness?"

"Dissatisfaction, bhante."

"And what is impermanent, dissatisfactory, and subject to change: is it fit to
regard it like this: 'This is mine, I am this, this is my self'?"

"No, bhante."

### The De-appropriation Refrain

"So then, bhikkhus, any kind of form there is, past, future, or present,
internal or external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near:
all form should be seen with right discernment as it has come to be: 'This is
not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.'

Any kind of felt experience there is, past, future, or present, internal or
external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near: all felt
experience should be seen with right discernment as it has come to be: 'This
is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.'

Any kind of recognition there is, past, future, or present, internal or
external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near: all
recognition should be seen with right discernment as it has come to be: 'This
is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.'

Any kind of putting-together activities there are, past, future, or present,
internal or external, coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near:
all putting-together activities should be seen with right discernment as they
have come to be: 'This is not mine, I am not this, this is not my self.'

Any kind of knowing there is, past, future, or present, internal or external,
coarse or subtle, inferior or superior, far or near: all knowing should be
seen with right discernment as it has come to be: 'This is not mine, I am not
this, this is not my self.'"

### Disenchantment, Fading, And Release

"Seeing this, bhikkhus, a learned noble disciple becomes disenchanted with
form, felt experience, recognition, putting-together activities, and knowing.

Being disenchanted, one fades. Through fading, one is released.

When it is released, there is the knowledge: 'It is released.'

One discerns: 'Birth is worn away, the spiritual life has been lived, what had
to be done has been done, there is no more of this state of being.'"

That is what the Buddha said.

The group of five bhikkhus were glad and welcomed what the Buddha said.

And while this exposition was being spoken, the minds of the group of five
bhikkhus were released from the outflows through not taking anything
personally.
