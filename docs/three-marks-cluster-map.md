# Three Marks Cluster Map

This note governs the repository's handling of the `anicca / dukkha / anattā`
family and its main formula-side supports.
It is not a general essay. It exists to stop the three-marks cluster from
drifting into stock Buddhist-English slogans or flattening its internal
progression.

## Core Distinctions

| Term | Default | Functional Role | Do Not Collapse Into |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `anicca` | `impermanent` | First mark in the progression and default instability language | temporary, merely changing |
| `dukkha` | `dissatisfaction` | Second mark in the progression and governing family default | suffering by default in every context |
| `anattā` | `not-self` | Third mark in the progression and anti-identification predicate | no-self slogan, nihilism |
| `saṅkhāra` | `putting things together` | Support term whose formula-side overrides must stay explicit | formations by habit |
| `saṅkhata` | `conditioned` | Result-side conditionedness term in the family | fabricated, made up |
| `dhamma` | `dhamma` | Governing headword that takes a narrow formula override in `sabbe dhammā anattā` | phenomenon in every context |

## Operational Reading Order

1. Keep the progression audible: impermanent -> dissatisfaction -> not-self.
2. If the text is in the classic three-marks formulae, preserve the formula
   overrides recorded in the cluster.
3. If the text is using the contemplation or perception forms, keep them
   coordinated with the headwords rather than rotating synonyms.

## Formula Guardrails

- `aniccā sabbasaṅkhārā` ->
  `all that has been put together is impermanent`
- `dukkhā sabbasaṅkhārā` ->
  `all that has been put together is unsatisfactory`
- `sabbe dhammā anattā` ->
  `all phenomena are not-self`
- `sabbe saṅkhatā aniccā` ->
  `all conditioned things are impermanent`
- `yaṃ aniccaṃ taṃ dukkhaṃ; yaṃ dukkhaṃ tad anattā` ->
  `what is impermanent is dissatisfaction; what is dissatisfaction is not-self`

Do not force those formula-side overrides back onto every ordinary occurrence of
`saṅkhāra`, `saṅkhata`, or `dhamma`.

## Family Logic

- `dukkha` remains governed here by the three-marks family while still
  coordinating with the four noble truths family.
- `anattā` remains a predicate against self-identification, not a metaphysical
  slogan.
- `saṅkhāra` and `dhamma` take formula-side overrides in this cluster; those
  overrides are controlled, not free-floating.

## Translator Guidance

- Do not let `dukkha` drift back to uncontrolled suffering-language in the
  three-marks family.
- Do not flatten `anattā` into nihilistic `no-self` rhetoric.
- Keep the family readable as a progression rather than three unrelated glosses.
- Keep the practice forms `aniccānupassanā`, `dukkhānupassanā`, and
  `anattānupassanā` coordinated with the headwords.

## Interface Guardrails

- Four noble truths interface:
  `dukkha` must remain coordinated with its truth-side handling without letting
  the truth framework swallow the three-marks progression.
- Formula interface:
  support terms can take narrow overrides inside the stock lines, but those
  overrides do not erase the headword policies.

## Main Drift Risks

- `anicca` -> temporary
- `dukkha` -> suffering everywhere
- `anattā` -> no-self slogan
- `saṅkhāra` formulae -> formations by habit
- `dhamma` formula override -> phenomenon everywhere
