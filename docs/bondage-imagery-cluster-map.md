# Bondage-Imagery Cluster Map

This note governs the repository's handling of the `ogha / yoga / gantha`
family.
It is not a general essay. It exists to stop concrete bondage imagery from
collapsing into one vague English blur.

## Core Distinctions

| Term | Default | Functional Role | Do Not Collapse Into |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `ogha` | `flood` | Overwhelming force that engulfs and is crossed over | generic bondage, `āsava`, obstacle-language |
| `yoga` | `yoke` | Yoking or harnessing force that keeps one under a burden | `saṃyojana`, generic attachment, modern yoga-language |
| `gantha` | `knot` | Knotting or fastening force that ties things up | `yoga`, `saṃyojana`, vague entanglement |
| `saṃyojana` | `fetter` | Canonical binding factor in awakening-stage analysis | every imagery term |
| `āsava` | `outflow` | Corruptive outflow or running-on residue | flood imagery, knot imagery |
| `anusaya` | `underlying tendency` | Latent persistence lying underneath experience | active imagery terms |
| `nīvaraṇa` | `distraction` | Active obstruction in practice | residue or bondage imagery |
| `kilesa` | `defilement` | Broad afflictive category | any narrower family by default |

## Operational Reading Order

1. If the text stresses being swept away, engulfed, or crossed over, prefer the `ogha` family.
2. If the text stresses being yoked, harnessed, or kept under a burden, prefer the `yoga` family.
3. If the text stresses knotting, fastening, or being tied up, prefer the `gantha` family.
4. Use `saṃyojana` when the text is in the fetter framework rather than metaphorical imagery.
5. Use `āsava` when the text is in the outflow framework rather than flood, yoke, or knot imagery.
6. Use `anusaya` when the text stresses latent persistence rather than active imagery.
7. Use `nīvaraṇa` when the text stresses present obstruction rather than residue or bondage imagery.

## Family Logic

- `ogha` is flood imagery.
- `yoga` is yoke imagery.
- `gantha` is knot imagery.
- `kāyagantha` is the specialized fourfold knot-family member set.
- These are related bondage metaphors, but they are not interchangeable.
- The residue family remains distinct:
  `āsava` runs on,
  `anusaya` lies underneath,
  `saṃyojana` fetters,
  `nīvaraṇa` obstructs,
  `kilesa` names the broad afflictive category.

## Translator Guidance

- Do not replace all three imagery terms with one safe English word such as `bondage`.
- Do not replace `ogha` with `outflow` just because both use fluid imagery.
- Do not replace `yoga` with `fetter` or generic `attachment` by habit.
- Do not leave `yoga` untranslated where English readers will hear modern spirituality or exercise associations.
- Do not replace `gantha` with `problem`, `attachment`, or vague `entanglement`.
- Let the concrete image do real work when the text does so.

## Release And Crossing Guidance

- `ogha` naturally pairs with crossing language. If the text is about crossing over, do not abstract the family away.
- `yoga` naturally pairs with `yogakkhema`, where the yoke image remains audible even in a fixed whole-expression rendering.
- `gantha` naturally suggests untying or release from being tied up; do not rewrite it as if it were merely another fetter list.
- When passages move into `virāga`, `vossagga`, `nirodha`, `khaya`, or `vimutti`, keep those release-family renderings distinct rather than letting them erase the imagery family that is being released from.

## Contrast Matrix

| If the text suggests... | Prefer | Avoid by Default |
| --- | --- | --- |
| being swept away, engulfed, crossed over | `flood` | `bondage`, `outflow`, obstacle-language |
| being yoked, harnessed, kept under a load | `yoke` | `fetter`, `attachment`, modern yoga-language |
| being knotted, fastened, tied up | `knot` | `attachment`, `problem`, vague entanglement |
| canonical stage-of-awakening binding set | `fetter` | imagery terms as substitutes |
| corruptive running-on or leakage | `outflow` | `flood`, `yoke`, `knot` |
| latent persistence beneath the surface | `underlying tendency` | active imagery terms |
| active meditation obstruction | `distraction` | residue or bondage imagery |
