# Idappaccayatā Practical Talk Profile

## Purpose

This document records the newly added `Pasted text.txt` source as a named
practical support profile for the repository's conditionality and
dependent-arising families.

The source is not treated here as a random appendix, and it is not promoted to
top-level authority on its own. It is being preserved because it gives a
strong, reusable practical presentation of `idappaccayatā` as the heart of the
teaching, the broad law of conditionality, and the basis for quenching
`dukkha`.

## Classification

This source is classified as a Buddhadāsa-line practical support profile.

When it is encoded in `authority_basis`, use `priority: buddhadasa-support`.

That means:

- it strongly aligns with the existing Buddhadāsa profile on conditionality,
  dependent arising, natural-law framing, not-self, emptiness, and here-now
  quenching of `dukkha`
- it can sharpen `notes`, `context_rules`, `authority_basis`, and anti-drift
  policy on nearby terms
- it does not outrank OSF house materials, Dhammarato, the core Buddhadāsa
  profile, or already stabilized repository defaults
- it should usually reinforce or clarify existing house direction rather than
  replace it

## Editorial Use

Use this profile mainly in three ways:

1. `ALIGN`

- strengthen `idappaccayatā` as the broad conditionality headword
- strengthen `paṭiccasamuppāda` as the suffering-specific practical application
  of that same law
- reinforce here-now handling of `dukkha`, `phassa`, `vedanā`, `taṇhā`,
  `upādāna`, and `bhava`

2. `TOLERATE ALTERNATE`

- preserve `law of nature` as a controlled explanatory phrase for
  `idappaccayatā` and nearby `dhamma`/`dhammatā` language
- preserve `just like that` as a controlled explanatory note for `tathatā`
- preserve the four-meanings `Dhamma` frame as a support-layer note rather than
  a blanket default translation rule

3. `REFUSE DRIFT`

- do not let provocative `god` rhetoric become default lexicon language
- do not let `paṭiccasamuppāda` drift into abstract cosmology or metaphysical
  world-building
- do not let `suññatā` and `anattā` drift into nihilism
- do not let `kamma` collapse into simplistic moral bookkeeping or fate

## High-Value Doctrinal Pressures

This profile is especially useful for the following repository directions:

- `idappaccayatā` as universal conditionality rather than a minor technical
  appendix
- `paṭiccasamuppāda` and `idappaccayatā` as one law viewed under different
  scopes: the former focused on the arising and quenching of `dukkha`, the
  latter on the broader lawfulness of conditions
- quenching of `dukkha` as inseparable from the lived `phassa -> vedanā ->
  taṇhā -> upādāna -> bhava` process
- `tathatā` as suchness or `just like that`, useful as anti-drift against
  appropriation and metaphysical overreach
- `suññatā` and `anattā` as empty of abiding self and appropriation rather than
  as nihilistic voidness
- `Dhamma` as nature, the law of nature, duty according to that law, and the
  fruit of acting in line with or against it

## Rhetorical Caution

The source repeatedly says that Buddhism has a `god` and that `idappaccayatā`
is that `god`.

The repository preserves the doctrinal point but not the rhetoric as a house
default.

Allowed use:

- note-level explanation that the source is naming the highest law of nature,
  not a person-like deity
- anti-drift warning against projecting theistic-person language into Buddhist
  doctrinal terms

Disallowed use:

- replacing `idappaccayatā` with `god`
- introducing person-like creator language into term defaults
- importing polemical contrast language as translator-facing house prose

## Scope Notes

This source is strongest where the repository needs practical anti-drift
clarification, not where it needs brand-new English defaults.

In particular, it is useful for:

- dependent-arising family coordination
- practical natural-law explanation
- immediate-life quenching of `dukkha`
- anti-metaphysical and anti-superstitious handling of `kamma`, `sukha`,
  `dukkha`, and sense-contact process

It is weaker as a source for:

- replacing already stabilized headword defaults
- importing sermon rhetoric directly into translator-facing policy
- resolving questions that require higher-priority OSF house wording
